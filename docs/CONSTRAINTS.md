# Project Constraints: Space Sim

Working design document for a KSP-lineage space exploration game. Captures locked decisions, open questions, and the implications between them. Read this before making any structural change to the project.

This document is the ground truth for the project. If a proposed change conflicts with anything here, the doc wins unless the doc is explicitly updated first.

## How to use this document

Items are tagged with one of three statuses:

- **LOCKED** — Foundation decisions. Changing these later is expensive. Don't reopen without strong evidence.
- **DEFAULT** — Working choice with clear rationale. Not yet permanent. Revisit if real evidence emerges.
- **OPEN** — Genuinely undecided. Must be resolved before the phase that depends on it begins.

If two constraints conflict, flag it and discuss before proceeding. Cascading mistakes here cost weeks.

## 1. Vision

A KSP-lineage space exploration game where the universe is large, time is real, engineering is honest, and the goal is to find out what's out there.

### Design pillars

- Engineering as the verb. Procedural parts, real orbital mechanics, satisfying physics. Players figure things out and the game respects that effort.
- Discovery as the meta-game. Hand-crafted home system teaches the game; procedural galaxy beyond provides endless variety. Information is the primary reward.
- Discovery as gameplay, grounded in real astrophysics. The universe is generated by physics and discovered through physics. Multiple detection methods (optical, infrared, radio, X-ray, gravitational, particle, direct sampling) each reveal different aspects of objects. Mystery is preserved by the limits of each method; depth is created by their combination. Players build models of the universe from incomplete data, refining as new instruments reveal new dimensions. This is the central exploration loop and the most differentiated feature of the game.
- Physics-grounded substrate. The universe operates by real (simplified) physics. Gameplay emerges from physical principles, not arbitrary mechanics. When designing any new feature, the question is "what physics does this connect to?" — if the answer is "nothing," the feature is probably wrong for this game.
- Atmospheric flight as first-class gameplay. Spaceplanes are a real vessel category, not a token nod. Juno-fidelity procedural aerodynamics with calculated lift/drag from procedural aero surfaces. Supersonic and hypersonic flight regimes in scope. Re-entry heating modeled thermally. Atmospheric flight is its own engineering discipline, not a launch detail to skip past.
- Time as a real dimension. Interstellar travel uses relativistic time-dilation. Journeys have consequences. The universe keeps moving while you're gone.
- Automation as a love letter to engineers. Once a problem is solved, it should never have to be re-executed by hand. Built-in Vizzy-style visual scripting.
- Selective realism. Realism that creates decisions is good. Realism that creates tedious repetition is bad. Failure modes, comms delay, mass margins yes. Manual ascent profiles every launch no.
- Asynchronous progression. Things happen while the player is doing other things — within bounded limits that prevent passive accumulation from creating optimization pressure. Research advances when scientists are assigned; supply lines deliver within network capacity; bases produce until depots fill, then idle. Every session produces visible forward motion (once the player has accumulated agency state — bases, missions, research, probes — to advance; the first hour has its own design, see section 7's `### First-hour experience`). The journey-gating principle: long absences change who and what, not how much.
- Layered depth. The same systems serve curious newcomers and engineering nerds. Defaults are friendly. Depth is available to those who want it.

### Target player types — all served simultaneously

- The curious newcomer: never played KSP. Wants to feel like an agency director. Success early, mystery to chase.
- The casual veteran: KSP background. Wants new content beyond what KSP offered. Wants the satisfaction of building rockets that work without months of mod configuration.
- The engineering nerd: Vizzy / kOS crowd. Wants honest simulation, deep tools, will spend 50 hours optimizing one launch profile.
- The explorer: wants mystery, new places, things to find. Engineering is the cost of admission to discovery.
- The completionist: wants the tech tree, the unlocks, the sense of building toward something. Factorio / Satisfactory mindset.

Design test: any new feature should serve at least three of these. Features that only serve one risk being scope inflation.

### Things we are explicitly NOT doing

- Full n-body physics. Patched conics is correct enough and infinitely more workable.
- Combat. Different game, different audience.
- Full-fidelity persistent galaxy-wide simulation. Only the player's current system is fully simulated; everything else uses light-tick simulation.
- Multiplayer in v1. Architecture leaves the door open but it's not a launch feature.
- Realistic-scale solar system as the default. Scaled-down KSP-style is default; realistic scale is an optional setting.
- Architectural base-building (Minecraft / Subnautica style). Bases are sites plus functional modules, not brick-by-brick construction.
- Hand-modeling hundreds of discrete parts. Procedural parts only.
- Deep base-building / factory game depth (Factorio / DSP). Resources serve the space game, not the other way around.
- Mining as a gameplay mode (manually driving rovers, etc.). Mining is a consequence of placing a module on a body.

### Tonal framing for game modes

Single-player and multiplayer are tonally different games sharing the same systems and universe. Single-player is the contemplative, time-dilated, asynchronous narrative — running an agency's space program across decades and lifetimes, with the dramatic weight of journeys that change everyone they touch. Multiplayer is the coordinated, shared, immediate cooperative or competitive experience — running a space program with other humans, where the universe stays in sync because you're in it together.

Both are valuable; neither is a diminished version of the other. Players choosing a mode are choosing a kind of story. This framing forecloses the natural complaint of "why can't multiplayer have time-dilation, you nerfed it" — multiplayer is a different experience by design intent, not by technical limitation.

The multiplayer architecture in section 4 (`### Multiplayer as shared universe`) makes the mechanical commitment that supports this tonal commitment: spatial warp instead of relativistic transit, shared game-time, no time-dilation. The tonal commitment here in the Vision section is the reason; the architecture there is the mechanism.

### Minimal-tycoon, rich-progression positioning

The game is not a tycoon. Resources serve gameplay decisions, not economic optimization.

Specifically:

- No money or universal currency. Resources are physical and located. You cannot convert hydrogen to credits and credits to research samples.
- No contracts or quests in the KSP sense. Goals come from the player, from anomalies, and from emergent crisis.
- Upgrades are capabilities (categorical improvements), not optimizations (numerical tweaks).
- Automation is rich within the constraints of bounded autonomous evolution. Supply lines run autonomously but within network capacity limits. Mining operations produce until depots fill. Research progresses when scientists are assigned. Vizzy scripts handle repeat tasks.
- Difficulty toggles available for tycoon-curious players: resource scarcity, mass margin requirements, life support requirements, crew salaries or training costs. Players who want economic puzzles can opt in.

The game is positioned as an expedition/exploration game where you run an agency's space program because you want to find out what's there. Resources, tech, and infrastructure are all in service of that exploration. The progression is real but it's not the point. The point is the journey.

### In-media-res starting state

**LOCKED:** The game begins with humanity already 50-60 years into its space age. The home system contains accumulated artifacts of prior space activity. The player's agency continues an existing tradition rather than founding one. The previous era reached significant accomplishments (a moon base, a Mars-equivalent outpost, a Voyager-equivalent probe, possibly a generation ship in transit) but ran out of momentum. The player's era is the current revival, pushing further than the previous era did.

**Starting infrastructure:**

- **Home planet:** fully-equipped ground capability (VAB, launch facilities, mission control, research labs, manufacturing).
- **Home moon:** partly-operational base inherited from the previous era. Capability for small craft assembly and local fuel production; not enough for major construction. Player can use, maintain, expand, or repurpose.
- **Next planet out (Mars-equivalent):** minimal research outpost with basic facilities. Smaller than the moon base. Player can expand or use as-is for limited operations.

Nothing exists in orbit at game start. Nothing exists at outer system bodies (beyond the player's eventual reach).

**Three-tier content structure for placed artifacts:**

- **Tier A (handcrafted unique artifacts):** 10-20 across the entire game at v1. Each designer-authored with specific intent, payoff, and gameplay around investigation. Examples: Voyager-equivalent at the heliopause, ISS-equivalent in orbit, Apollo-equivalent landing sites on the home moon, planetary lander remains on the Mars-equivalent, a generation ship en route to a nearby star with unknown status, a wormhole or equivalent deep-space anomaly known but unreachable with starting tech, an unexplained signal source. Concentrated in the home system with a few in the local interstellar neighborhood.
- **Tier B (handcrafted classes filled procedurally):** 6-10 distinct classes producing many instances. Defunct satellites, deep-space probes, mining outposts on asteroids, communication relays, abandoned bases, derelict ships, old radio sources at nearby stars. Each class has rich variation parameters. The player encounters many; learns the class; notices exceptions.
- **Tier C (ambient procgen texture):** Emerges from the procgen pipeline. Background radio sources at every star. Debris distributions in asteroid belts. Old mission traces. Density everywhere; specific design attention not required.

The player's agency is the continuation of the previous era's space program. The first transmissions can acknowledge this: review meetings reference Voyager-2 telemetry, ISS retirement decisions, the laser propulsion research program. The world has history; the player is its current author.

**Prior-era artifacts as research opportunities.** The Tier A handcrafted artifacts (Voyager-equivalent, ISS-equivalent, Apollo sites, the deep-space anomaly) are not just narrative weight — they are investigation opportunities. Each Tier A artifact opens specific research questions when encountered. Retrieving the Voyager-equivalent's recorded data is a research project. Investigating why the generation ship lost contact is a research project. The artifacts seed the early research-as-logistics gameplay (see section 5's `### Research as logistics-driven question-answering`) by providing concrete first questions the player can investigate.

**The opening directive: rescue mission.** The default game-start presents the player with an immediate operational situation. One of the inherited assets — a small base, a long-flight crew vessel, or an outer-system station — has a critical life support failure with crew aboard. The opening directive: mount a rescue mission. This establishes immediate stakes, demonstrates the game's core systems (mission planning, crew management, supply logistics, real consequences), and reveals the world as one where things are happening regardless of the player's attention.

The opening directive is the default but not the only game-start option. Configurable starting conditions (`### Configurable starting conditions` above) allow players to opt for alternative openings: pure sandbox (no directive, free to do anything), ongoing observatory operations (no immediate crisis, focus on discovery), engineering challenge (specific design problem to solve). The rescue directive is the engaged-narrative default; other modes serve other player preferences.

Beyond the opening, exploration objectives continue to surface throughout play — anomalies detected, prior-era artifacts found, opportunities to push capability further. Most player work emerges from these ongoing surfaces rather than from authored objectives.

### Logistics-not-tech as primary progression gating

**LOCKED:** All parts are available from game start. The player can build whatever the parts vocabulary allows immediately. Progression is not tech unlocks — it is the physics of getting payloads where they need to go.

The rocket equation is the gate. Real engines produce real thrust at real Isp. Real tanks have real mass fractions. Real structural elements have real masses. The math determines what works. No single rocket can lift an interstellar-capable craft because the math does not allow it. The player can launch what physics allows; physics determines reach.

**Infrastructure progression as the path** (six player-experience stages over hours of play — these are distinct from the development build phases in section 9):

- **Stage 1 (hours 1-10):** Home system development. Reach the moon, reach Mars-equivalent, use existing infrastructure, develop additional small outposts.
- **Stage 2 (hours 10-30):** Build an orbital VAB. Requires many launches to deliver components. Once built, enables construction of craft too large to launch in one piece.
- **Stage 3 (hours 30-60):** Interplanetary infrastructure. Substantial bases on multiple bodies. Asteroid mining. Surface VABs at other bodies enable in-situ construction. Laser propulsion array begins construction at home.
- **Stage 4 (hours 60-100):** Tier 1 interstellar. Laser array operational. Small one-way sail probes to nearby stars. Decades of game time per mission.
- **Stage 5 (hours 100-200):** Tier 2 interstellar. Larger sail ships, possibly slow-boat colony ships, generation ships.
- **Stage 6 (hours 200+):** Tier 3 interstellar. Destination infrastructure (laser arrays at target stars) enables two-way travel. Full interstellar civilization gameplay opens.

**The KSP veteran case:** a skilled player moves through Stage 1 faster (maybe 3-5 hours instead of 10) but cannot skip Stage 2 because orbital VAB construction is physical work requiring many launches and assembly time. By Stage 4, veterans and new players are at comparable pacing. Same game, different speeds.

**The discipline this requires:** engine numbers must be honest. Tank mass fractions must be realistic. Structural masses must match real engineering. The temptation to fudge for gameplay must be resisted because the gating mechanism depends on the physics being real.

**Why this matters.** Solves the tech-tree-vs-sandbox tension. Solves the pacing problem (players progress naturally). Solves the veteran-vs-new-player problem (same constraints for everyone). Solves the home-investment problem (players who want to push outward must develop home infrastructure first). Produces progression that feels meaningful because every accomplishment is real engineering.

**What the tech tree still gates.** Parts are unlocked from start; the tech tree continues to gate capabilities and infrastructure that aren't parts: observatory instrument tiers, specialty propulsion infrastructure (laser sail arrays and beyond), advanced manufacturing facilities (orbital VAB, surface VABs at other bodies), and research samples as categorical inventory. The tech tree's behavior is configurable at game creation (see `### Configurable starting conditions` below); the default has tech gating capabilities while parts are universal from start.

### Configurable starting conditions

**LOCKED:** The game has one mode. Players configure their starting conditions and progression rules at game creation rather than choosing between "sandbox" and "career" as separate modes.

**Configuration options at game start:**

- Starting tech level (everything unlocked / standard starting set / minimal starting set / custom)
- Progression source (research from missions / research from observatory / both / neither — everything unlocks immediately)
- Whether anomalies and discoveries gate specific advanced tech (yes / no)
- Difficulty toggles on all existing axes (already locked)

Same systems, different starting points. A player who wants full sandbox sets "everything unlocked, no progression." A player who wants progression sets "minimal start, research gated." Players in the middle set whatever they want.

This is the same pattern as the locked "difficulty as independent toggles" pillar. Difficulty is not a mode; it is configurable settings. Tech availability and progression follow the same pattern.

### Six play styles supported by the design

The design supports six play styles through depth in existing systems rather than through separate features:

- **The engineer.** Plays to build interesting craft. Procedural parts system with rich variation. Vizzy scripting deepens engineering.
- **The explorer.** Plays to find things. Detection ecosystem, 8-stage discovery progression, 90/9/1 anomaly distribution, starting artifacts.
- **The colonist.** Plays to build the home program. Base modules, supply line systems, bounded autonomous evolution.
- **The strategist.** Plays for long-game planning. Tech progression through engineering gates, infrastructure choices, resource allocation.
- **The narrator.** Plays for emergent story. Director perspective, named crew with time-dilation effects, program history.
- **The completionist.** Plays to find or build everything. Persistent catalog, bounded procgen variety.

Players mix freely. Most engage with several. Variety is emergent from system depth, not added as separate features.

### Design strategy commitment

**LOCKED:** The design follows two strategies in combination: refused breadth (Strategy 5: explicit non-goals protect focused depth, KSP-shaped) plus deep vocabulary producing emergent variety (Strategy 1: one rich system produces many activities, Minecraft-shaped).

**What this means in practice.** Variety comes from making existing systems deep enough to support multiple play styles, not from adding parallel systems. Every time the temptation arises to add a new system for variety, the response is to deepen existing systems first.

**Strategies the design does not follow:**

- Multiple competent systems integrated by world (Fallout-shaped). Would require many shallow systems; loses focus.
- Mechanical foundation supporting emergent social play (EVE-shaped). Multiplayer is post-v1 and not the core loop.
- Curated activity rotation (GTA-shaped). Requires massive content budgets.

**Specifically rejected from this design:** Combat as gameplay. Economy as meta-game. Quest systems / mission contracts. RPG mechanics on crew. Social mechanics in single-player. Discrete activity types ("exploration mode" vs "engineering mode" as separate gameplay shapes).

**The discipline:** when implementation reveals "missing" capability, the first instinct is to deepen existing systems. Most missing things are insufficient depth in existing systems.

### Dramatic shapes the game produces

**LOCKED:** The game does not author plots. It designs systems that produce certain dramatic shapes naturally when the player engages with them. Implementation discipline: each system should support multiple dramatic shapes. The work is not writing plots; it is ensuring the systems are honest enough to produce these shapes when players engage with them.

**The dramatic shapes the design produces:**

- **The rescue.** Crew or vessel in trouble. Player mounts rescue.
- **The arrival.** Long-launched probe arrives. Crew returns from long mission. Colony ship reaches destination.
- **The discovery.** Anomaly resolves into something significant. Hero finds happen.
- **The reach.** Player accomplishes something at the edge of capability. First orbit, first Mun landing, first interstellar launch.
- **The disaster.** Catastrophic loss within a running program. Reactor failure, crew loss, infrastructure destruction.
- **The pivot.** Plans meet reality and stop working. Player adjusts strategy.
- **The long arc.** Decades-old investment comes to a head. Generation ship arrives. Slow research project pays off.
- **The cost.** Real choice between two desired things. Save crew or complete mission. Invest in interstellar or develop home.
- **The legacy.** Long-playthrough accumulation. Discoveries named after the player. Infrastructure that outlasts a generation.
- **The encounter with prior humanity.** Finding evidence of what came before. Voyager-equivalent retrieved, Apollo site visited, generation ship's fate determined.
- **The reach that creates the problem.** Success that opens new questions. First interstellar probe arrives and reveals something requiring different capability.

**Specific supports needed in implementation:** telemetry legibility, time visibility, persistent history, real consequences from real choices, arrival events as moments, catalog and discovery surfacing, anomaly resolution tiered by significance, prior-humanity artifacts that pay off.

### World-is-alive-via-player-decisions principle

**LOCKED:** The world's autonomous behavior during player absence is predominantly the consequence of player decisions, not random events. A supply line failure fails because of how the player designed the route. A colony that grows beyond expectations grows because of how the player configured its automation. The texture of the world while the player is away is the player's own creation.

**Why this matters.** Random events feel arbitrary ("your tanker hit a micrometeoroid" with no player input). Decision consequences feel earned ("your tanker design has insufficient redundancy to handle off-nominal conditions"). The design leans heavily toward the second.

This principle applies to:

- Supply line outcomes (route failures trace to craft design choices)
- Base evolution (autonomous expansion within bounded rules the player configured)
- Research progress (advances when scientists were assigned, paused otherwise)
- Crew lives (relationships and aging follow from the missions and assignments the player chose)
- Anomaly investigations (auto-completing during absence based on the resources the player committed)

Random events still exist (procedural failure modes, stellar weather, mechanical wear) but they operate within the system the player designed and they manifest as expressions of design choices rather than as arbitrary interruptions.

### Home system as load-bearing design

**LOCKED:** The home system must be rich enough to carry 30-60 hours of player engagement before interstellar capability arrives. This makes home-system design effort load-bearing rather than decorative.

**What this requires:**

Four bodies receive intensive hand-craft: the home planet, the home moon, the Mars-equivalent (inner outpost world), and the Saturn-equivalent (outer-system anchor with multiple hand-tuned moons). These four are where Tier A artifacts concentrate, where landmarks are hand-placed, and where the procgen pipeline is run with deliberate parameter choices plus designer overrides at the surface-feature level.

All other home system bodies receive hand-tuned 14-stage parameters (deliberate parameter choices, no random seed) but no intensive artifact placement. They feel like specific places (named, characterized, distinct) rather than procgen-galaxy-generic, but they don't carry the same density of designed content as the four intensive bodies.

The intensive-craft scope per body:

- **Home planet:** fully designed surface (continents, oceans, terrain), hand-placed landmarks, multiple Tier A artifacts (ISS-equivalent in orbit, prior-era ground sites, the active home base infrastructure). The first hour of gameplay happens here.
- **Home moon:** inherited base from prior era (functional but limited), Apollo-equivalent landing sites with prior-era hardware, hand-placed landmarks, designed terrain features. First major mission target.
- **Mars-equivalent:** minimal inherited outpost, prior-era lander remains scattered across the surface (Tier A artifacts), atmospheric characterization that rewards investigation. Second major mission target.
- **Saturn-equivalent system:** the gas giant itself with rings (designer-tuned for this body, but rings remain a galaxy-wide procgen feature emerging from parameter combinations at other gas giants too — Saturn is not the only ringed body in the galaxy), 5-10 hand-tuned moons across diverse characters, an Enceladus-equivalent with subsurface water ocean (effectively infinite for gameplay purposes; extraction does not deplete the resource meaningfully across a normal playthrough — extractable via nuclear excavation or laser-array vaporization, both producing crater-primitive surface features per `### Crater primitives and bounded terrain modification` in §6), an existing Saturn-orbit satellite from the prior era with sensor data showing the Enceladus water ocean (clue-trail for early investigation), a moon with an inherited rover (still transmitting limited telemetry, recoverable via mission). The deep-space anomaly and its outer-system narrative weight live in the Saturn system.

**Saturn rings implementation depth.** Rings render as visual ring structure plus a low-detail particle field. Vessels passing through experience light particle interactions but not granular per-particle physics. Rings are mineable as a distributed resource zone — specialized craft can extract ring material in bulk, with mining mechanics similar to asteroid mining but tuned for the ring environment. Mineable rings produce no terrain modification effects (no crater primitives from ring mining; rings are spatial volumes, not surfaces). The same implementation applies to procgen ring systems elsewhere in the galaxy.

**Why this matters.** The dramatic weight of time-dilation depends on home being a place the player has invested in. Investment happens naturally through extended time in the home frame. The four intensive bodies carry the time-investment weight; the other bodies provide variety and exploration targets without requiring designer time per body. This makes the home system implementation tractable while preserving the design's intent.

### Off-world mining as interstellar gating

**LOCKED:** The home planet does not produce enough resources to build interstellar infrastructure. Specific resources for endgame propulsion require off-world sources. This is natural infrastructure gating from physics, not arbitrary unlock barriers.

**Resources requiring off-world sourcing for endgame capabilities:**

- **Helium-3** for fusion propulsion: lunar regolith, gas giant atmospheres. Phase 7+ resource per `### Resource set` in §5.
- **Volatiles in bulk** for laser-sail-tier propellant requirements: outer-system ice bodies, Enceladus-equivalent water ocean, asteroid water.
- **Rare earths in mission-grade quantities:** specific asteroid bodies, certain inner-system rocky moons.
- **Fissile material in expansion quantities:** the home planet supplies starting fissile capacity; expansion-tier infrastructure (large reactor banks, mass driver power, advanced propulsion test programs) requires asteroid-belt or outer-system fissile sourcing.

**The progression cascade this produces:**

The player can build small-scale capability with home-sourced resources. Stage 1-2 logistics (commit 010) use home resources entirely. By Stage 3, asteroid mining and outer-system ice extraction become real options — small at first, then scaling. Stage 4 interstellar propulsion (laser sail one-way) requires the laser array, which requires fissile expansion that requires asteroid mining. Stage 5-6 interstellar capability requires bulk volatiles from outer-system ice extraction or Enceladus-style targeted nuking/laser-stripping operations. The infrastructure cascade is real engineering, not abstract tech unlocks.

The Saturn-equivalent system (above) is the natural late-game industrial expansion target — moons rich in volatiles, ring material accessible for mining, Enceladus-equivalent water ocean as a high-value target. Players develop Saturn-system infrastructure as part of building interstellar capability.

### Forgiving restart philosophy

**LOCKED:** The game's weight is in decisions, not in mistakes. Quicksave and autosave are abundant. Reloading a save fully reverts state to the save's canonical state — including all running counters (accumulated radiation doses, design launch counts, supply line states, crew tolerances, everything that's stored in the save). The only things that "persist" across save/reload are the player's learned knowledge and the design files (which are stored separately from saves per `### Mode-portable designs and templates` in §3).

**The intent:** Players who make a mistake — botched landing, mis-timed launch window, crew loss from misjudged radiation exposure — can reload and try again. Players who make a deliberate decision and want it to stick can play forward. The game does not punish mistakes by making them irreversible; it makes decisions meaningful by making them carry forward in chosen play.

**Save mechanics:**

- **Quicksave:** Player-initiated, instant, frequent. A single key invokes quicksave; another key invokes quickload. The quicksave slot is overwritten on each new quicksave (no quicksave history; use named saves for history).
- **Autosave:** Game-initiated, instant, multiple slots. Triggers on:
- Every 10-30 minutes of real-time play (player-configurable interval)
- Mission launch
- Mission arrival at destination
- Successful landing
- Crew transfer / EVA completion
- New body characterization stage achieved (Stage 1-8 transitions in `### Discovery progression — eight stages` in §4)
- Research project completion (`### Research as logistics-driven question-answering` in §5)
- Catastrophic failure (so the player has a clean revert point right before the disaster)

**Saves in simulator state are sim-state only.** The simulator is a forked branch of the real game's state, not the real game itself. Quicksave and autosave both work inside the simulator — the player can save mid-sim, reload to revert in-sim mistakes, and the autosave timer continues during sim sessions. But these saves live in the simulator's branch: they capture sim state, not real state. Exiting the simulator reverts to the real game's pre-simulator state regardless of how many saves were taken in sim. Sim-state saves are discarded on exit.

This means the simulator is fully savable for in-sim experimentation (try a docking maneuver, save before the tricky part, reload to retry, save again before exit-trajectory burn) without contaminating real game state. The save/reload discipline within sim works the same as in the real game; only the branch is different.

- **Named saves:** Player-initiated, instant, slots manage manually. For preserving specific states across longer play (start of a campaign, before a major decision, end of a mission). Named saves in sim state are also sim-only and discarded on exit.

**Why abundant saves matter.** The game has multiple sources of irreversible-feeling consequences if saves were rare — crew dies in critical condition, a single-launch interstellar probe is mis-targeted, an exploration mission fails halfway. With abundant saves, these are recoverable mistakes. The player retries the difficult thing; learns the difficult thing; succeeds the difficult thing. Without abundant saves, the same situations become punishing rather than instructive.

The Fallout 4 reference is intentional: that game's abundant-save culture supported deep mechanical engagement without punishing experimentation. The same model applies here.

## 2. Foundation (LOCKED unless noted)

These decisions cascade through everything. Treat as architectural commitments.

### Engine

**LOCKED:** Unity, C#, URP (Universal Render Pipeline).

Rationale: massive asset ecosystem, strong AI-assistance coverage, all relevant prior art available. URP (not HDRP) because we want to scale from mobile to PC from the same codebase. Custom engines (RocketWerkz's Brutal, etc.) make sense for multi-game studios amortizing R&D; they do not make sense for a single project.

Implication: Unity's scene-with-fixed-origin assumption is the main thing we have to architect around. Floating origin handles this.

### Coordinate system

**LOCKED:** 64-bit double-precision world coordinates (Unity.Mathematics double3). Floating origin for rendering.

Rationale: 32-bit floats lose precision at planetary scale. Unity rendering needs the camera near origin. Floating origin shifts the rendered world to keep the camera near origin; double-precision world coordinates store the authoritative position.

Floating origin shift threshold: 50 km default. The threshold is data, not a constant — globally per save, with single-value scope at any moment, tunable later by body parameters or difficulty settings.

Shifts only occur at sim-tick boundaries, never mid-tick. The sim-tick controller owns the shift operation; nothing else may shift the origin. When a shift is pending, no system may mutate PhysX state between the end of one tick and the application of the shift — the shift is atomic from PhysX's perspective. All Transforms in the scene translate atomically; PhysX sees a clean teleport of the whole world.

When a shift occurs, floating-origin-aware systems receive a callback or event with the shift vector. Float-precision-cached positions (rendering, particle systems, decals) update in response. Double-precision authoritative state is unaffected by definition — double-precision world coordinates don't move when the rendering origin moves.

Rendering precision at distances of 10–50 km from origin is solved in the rendering layer (camera-relative rendering or logarithmic depth), not in the physics layer. This is Phase 4 work, not Phase 1.

A known refactor path exists if this approach reveals problems we can't solve: vessel-local PhysX scenes, where each PhysX-active vessel simulates in a coordinate space anchored at the vessel itself, with world position stored in the authoritative layer. The interface to the rest of the game — vessels at world coordinates, forces applied, results produced — is invariant across the current approach and the refactor, so the choice is reversible.

### Reference frame hierarchy

**LOCKED:** Hierarchical frames — Sun, planet, moon, vessel. Explicit transitions at sphere-of-influence boundaries.

A vessel exists in exactly one SOI at a time. When it crosses an SOI boundary, its orbital elements are re-rooted in the new frame.

### Orbital mechanics

**LOCKED:** Patched conics within stellar systems. Kepler orbital elements as the canonical representation for on-rails vessels.

Propagation uses Kepler's equation (iterative solve). Position/velocity state vectors derived from elements when needed.

### Physics architecture

**LOCKED:** Three-mode system.

- **PhysX-active:** Unity PhysX for physics-active vessels (within atmosphere, near surface, under thrust).
- **Kepler-rails:** analytic propagation for on-rails vessels (stable orbit, not under thrust, no atmosphere).
- **Interstellar-cruise:** for vessels between stellar systems. Straight-line trajectory between origin and destination. The mode's behavior is parameterized by game mode and propulsion tier: in single-player, vessel proper time advances at 1 divided by gamma (relativistic transit); in multiplayer, proper time always equals game time and the vessel traverses interstellar distance at a configured warp velocity for its propulsion tier (spatial warp, no relativistic effects). Per-vessel proper-time clocks exist in both modes as data; their behavior is determined by mode.

Transitions between modes are the highest-bug-density boundary in the project. Design carefully. Test exhaustively. Conditions for entering Kepler-rails: vessel above atmospheric threshold, not thrusting, time-warp above a threshold rate. Conditions for entering interstellar-cruise: vessel has departed an SOI on an escape trajectory aimed at another stellar system (player-confirmed, not automatic).

Interstellar-cruise arrival semantics are event-driven and branch by vessel type. Tier 2 (laser sail one-way, flyby-only) arrival is a single analytic event: the resolution computes synthetic flyby geometry from arrival velocity, aim point, and destination star's gravitational parameter; derives which bodies the probe got close enough to for useful observation and at what observation tier; schedules the science-data-return comms message (delivered after light-speed comms delay); marks the vessel as lost. The vessel never enters Kepler-rails or PhysX-active in the destination system. It is never propagated. It is a math event producing an information payload. Tier 3 (laser sail two-way, decelerated by destination array) arrival inserts the vessel into Kepler-rails in the destination system, with orbital elements determined by arrival geometry — typically a capture orbit around the destination star. Tier 3 arrival also advances proper-time and home-time clocks for the dilation payoff and triggers narrative/notification events. After insertion, a tier 3 vessel behaves as any Kepler-rails vessel.

Architectural principle this clarifies, applying game-wide: the simulation resolves events; the presentation layer visualizes them; they are separable concerns. Tier 2 arrival has a Phase 5/6 visualization (a "ghost flyby" trace through the destination system with closest-approach markers) that is a UI deliverable, not a simulation deliverable. The simulation's correctness does not depend on whether the visualization exists.

Save-load handles interstellar arrivals identically to any other event in the priority queue (see Mode transitions and event scheduling below). Time advances on load; arrival events whose times fall within the gap fire in time order; their resolutions run. The asynchronous-progression machinery handles interstellar arrivals through the same path as research completions, supply line deliveries, and base resource ticks.

### Mode transitions and event scheduling

**LOCKED:** Mode transitions and time-driven events are scheduled through a single analytic event-prediction priority queue, owned by the sim-tick controller.

Each Kepler-rails vessel maintains an analytically-computed next-event time. Time-warp advances by `min(tick × warp_rate, time_to_next_event_across_all_vessels)`. The warp controller maintains a sorted priority queue of vessel next-event times. Authority over the queue lives in the sim-tick controller — the same controller that owns floating-origin shifts and authoritative-state sampling from PhysX.

Event types predicted in Phase 1: atmospheric entry, surface impact, SOI boundary crossing (entering and exiting), scheduled-burn arrival, interstellar-cruise arrival. Orbital intersection with other tracked vessels is deferred to Phase 5/6 when collision/rendezvous gameplay arrives.

Event predictors are pure functions of orbital and trajectory state. Given an orbit and current time, a predictor returns the next event time or +∞ if no event in the foreseeable future. No hidden state, no caches that can drift. Cache the result against vessel state; invalidate when state changes.

Event predictors have a detection-aggressiveness parameter. Phase 1 ships with two values, exposed later via the difficulty toggle system: a pragmatic default that aggressively detects atmospheric entry and surface impact (missing these is catastrophic for the player) and applies standard root-finding to clear SOI crossings; and a strict mode that applies aggressive detection to all event types including SOI grazing (near-tangent cases). Under default settings, some SOI grazes will be missed — this is a deliberate trade in favor of predictor performance. Players who want full physics accuracy can enable the physics fidelity toggle to receive strict-mode behavior across all predictors.

Phase 1 ships the pragmatic version of each predictor. The difficulty toggle that exposes the strict-mode value becomes available in Phase 5/6 alongside the broader difficulty toggle system from section 8. The predictor architecture supports the toggle from Phase 1; only the user-facing setting is deferred.

When a vessel's state changes — new maneuver node, Vizzy command affecting orbit at PhysX-active mode exit, mode transition, SOI re-rooting — its next-event time is recomputed and its queue entry is updated.

When a warp step lands at an event time, the event resolves at exactly the event time, then warp continues or halts according to player preferences. Halt-on-event-type is a player-facing setting: auto-drop warp on atmospheric entry by default; SOI crossings resolved silently by default.

The "warp to next event" UI feature is a view onto the same priority queue. One system, one queue, multiple consumers.

Adding a new event type later means writing its predictor function and adding it to the event-type enum. The priority queue and dispatch are invariant. This is the extensibility hook that lets orbital-intersection arrive in Phase 5/6 without architectural change.

### Time-warp in single-player

**LOCKED:** Continuous time-warp is the primary single-player time advancement mechanic. The player accelerates time at a multiplier (1×, 10×, 100×, up to a maximum), watches the simulation advance, and drops warp when something requires attention or when they reach their target moment. The visual idiom (clock spinning, orbits progressing on the map, transmissions arriving in the comms log) is preserved.

**Maximum time-warp rate in single-player is uniformly high (target: 100,000×) everywhere in the game.** There is no contextual cap based on whether the vessel is in-system or interstellar. The "warp defeats FTL" problem is solved structurally by real-scale interstellar distances (see `### Interstellar distances` above), not by limiting warp rates.

**"Warp to next event" is a navigation feature built on the analytic event prediction priority queue** from `### Mode transitions and event scheduling`. The player can request "advance directly to the next significant event," and the game jumps time forward to that moment. This is different from manually cranking warp and waiting — it is an explicit request to skip to the next interesting moment. The UI feature reads from the same priority queue used by the simulation; both consume one source.

Free time-warp is single-player only. In multiplayer, time is shared across all players via a coordinated time multiplier (see section 4 `### Multiplayer as shared universe`).

### Time representation

**LOCKED:** Double-precision seconds since epoch as canonical game time. Separate real-time, game-time, and warp-rate. Per-vessel proper time clock for relativistic effects.

Game time advances independently of real time. Warp rate is a multiplier. Each vessel additionally tracks its own proper time. Most of the time proper time equals game time; they diverge only during relativistic interstellar coast in single-player. In multiplayer, proper time always equals game time because multiplayer interstellar transit uses spatial warp, not relativistic transit.

### Save format implications

**LOCKED:** All vessels, bases, supply lines, research projects, and observations are serializable.

On save load: advance all asynchronous systems forward by (current time minus last save time), subject to the bounded-autonomous-evolution rules from section 4's `### Home system evolves autonomously` subsection. Interstellar-cruise vessels advance their proper-time clocks. Any vessels that arrived during the gap transition to Kepler-rails in their destination system per the rules in section 2's `### Physics architecture` subsection. Bases produce and consume resources for the elapsed period, but production stops when depot capacity is reached (the base idles until capacity is freed). Research projects advance only for time periods when scientists were assigned (abandoned projects pause). Supply line shipments arrive on schedule, within network throughput limits.

This is the architectural backbone of asynchronous progression. Every system that produces or consumes anything over time MUST be representable as (state at time T, rate function) so it can be advanced analytically rather than by simulation.

**Save files are mode-locked at creation.** Saves are tied to their game mode at creation and cannot be transferred between single-player and multiplayer.

- Single-player saves with relativistic-divergent proper-time clocks cannot be loaded as multiplayer sessions.
- Multiplayer saves cannot be converted to single-player.
- This is a hard architectural constraint that affects save format design.

**Explicit non-goal:** there is no feature for importing single-player crew, vessels, or progress state into multiplayer (or vice versa). Saves are mode-locked at creation. Players who want to play with friends start a new multiplayer save together. Mode-portability applies to designs and templates only, not to runtime state — see section 3's "Mode-portable designs and templates" subsection.

**Save mechanics implementation:**

- Quicksave and autosave both produce complete save files (full state snapshots, not deltas). On save load, the file is the canonical state per the existing save-format rules above.
- Quicksave slot is a single dedicated file path; overwritten on each quicksave. Quickload reads from this file.
- Autosave slots cycle through a configurable number of rotating files (default: 5 slots rotating; player can configure). Each autosave creates a new file in the next slot, overwriting the oldest.
- Named saves create player-titled files with no automatic overwriting.
- All save files are mode-locked at creation per existing save-format rules; mode-portable designs are stored separately.
- **Simulator-state saves use a separate sim-branch save space.** When the player is in simulator mode (`### Sandbox-as-simulator integrated in Mission Control` in §7), quicksave / autosave / named saves all operate on the sim-branch save space rather than the main save space. Sim-branch saves are discarded when the player exits the simulator. The implementation uses a separate file path namespace for sim-branch saves; on simulator exit, the sim-branch namespace is cleared. The main save space remains exactly as it was when the player entered the simulator.

Performance budget: quicksave should complete within 2 seconds on target hardware. Autosaves can take longer (5-10 seconds is acceptable since they don't interrupt active gameplay).

### Solar system scale

**DEFAULT:** 1/8 real scale. Home Kerbin-equivalent ~800 km radius, surface gravity ~9.8 m/s², delta-v to orbit ~4,000 m/s.

Rationale: slightly more demanding than KSP's 1/10 scale but well within reach for new players given the locked information-legibility design pillar (delta-v indicators, mass margins, clear failure explanations). The goal is not to make the game harder — it is to give mass margins enough weight that they create real engineering decisions while staying approachable enough that the first-hour design targets remain achievable. KSP-tuned intuitions are almost right at 1/8 but slightly off, giving experienced players something new to learn while preserving the bulk of their transferable skill.

Difficulty toggle presets:

- **Casual (1/10):** KSP-style scale for the most approachable experience.
- **Standard (1/8):** default.
- **Realistic (1/1):** hardcore real-Earth scale.

Players can configure custom scales if they want intermediate values; presets cover the named experiences. All body parameters remain data (not hardcoded constants) per the existing architectural commitment; the scale change is to the default parameter set, not to the architecture.

### Interstellar distances

**DEFAULT:** 1× to 5× real-world distances between stars. Interstellar distances are not scaled down to match in-system scale.

Rationale: in single-player, vessels traverse interstellar space at real relativistic velocities; real-scale distances produce realistic-feeling journey durations naturally. A tier 2 probe to a Proxima-equivalent star takes real-time hours to days at max warp, gated by physical distance rather than by an imposed cap.

In-system distances remain compressed at the chosen solar system scale (see above). The two scales are different because the gameplay contexts are different: in-system requires tractable distances for active mission play; interstellar requires vast distances for journey weight. Real space exhibits exactly this ratio — solar systems are tiny compared to interstellar gaps — so this is more realistic than uniform scaling, not less.

In multiplayer (see section 4 `### Multiplayer as shared universe`), spatial warp at configured velocities operates over the same interstellar distances; the journey duration is determined by warp velocity rather than relativistic transit, but the distances themselves are identical to single-player.

### Scaling discipline

**LOCKED:** Scale what is necessary for gameplay tractability. Keep real what is necessary for physics consistency. Document where the seams are.

**What scales:**

- Bodies and inter-body distances. 1/8 default. Body parameters (radius, mass, gravity) tuned to produce Earth-like surface gravity and reasonable delta-v budgets. Density is unrealistically high; gameplay is consistent.
- Atmospheric depth. Atmospheres are proportionally deeper than real (KSP-shaped) to make atmospheric flight gameplay-relevant.
- Star gravitational parameters for orbital mechanics. Tuned so orbital velocities and periods at scaled distances produce reasonable mission times.

**What does not scale:**

- Craft and craft components. Full real-size with real physics. A rocket is 50m tall; an astronaut is 1.8m. The rocket equation is honest using real engine performance and real tank mass fractions.
- Interstellar distances. Real (1× to 5× real) between stars.
- Stellar luminosity, temperature, and spectral parameters. Physically real because they affect detection signatures and habitability calculations. Solar flux at the home planet tuned to Earth-equivalent; scales with real distance from star from there.
- Communication delay. Real light-speed delay. In-system delays are short; interstellar delays are years.

**Implementation seams:**

- The transition from in-system (scaled) to interstellar (unscaled) at SOI boundaries.
- Atmospheric physics using real equations within tuned atmospheres.
- Detection signature computation using real photon physics from real luminosities.

Without explicit scaling discipline, implementation can subtly fudge in ways that compound. With it, every implementation decision can be checked against the principle.

### Target platforms

**DEFAULT:** PC primary at v1. Mobile-capable architecture from day one. Mobile ship target deferred until after vertical slice exists.

Rationale: mobile-first discipline forces good architecture (memory, LOD, draw calls) even if we never ship mobile. URP supports both. Architecturally cheap; shipping commitment can wait.

### Multiplayer architecture preparation

**LOCKED:** Architecture supports multiplayer from day one. Multiplayer is NOT a v1 shipping feature.

Implementation requirements that apply from Phase 1:

- Deterministic simulation: same inputs produce same outputs. No reliance on undefined ordering, uninitialized memory, or wall-clock dependencies.
- State-replicable: all game state is serializable. No hidden mutable state, no Unity-specific non-determinism (Random.value without explicit seeds, GetComponent ordering dependencies, etc.).
- Authority-aware: every state change has an explicit "who decided this" attribution, even in single-player. Foundation for future host/client architecture.
- Fixed-timestep simulation tick, decoupled from rendering.

The determinism requirement applies to authoritative state, not to the PhysX-active envelope. Determinism is required for all authoritative state outside PhysX-active mode: Kepler-rails propagation, interstellar-cruise, Vizzy VMs, resource/research/supply-line simulation, RNG-driven failure modes, all save/load state. Inside PhysX-active mode, simulation is non-deterministic by design. Future multiplayer for nearby-physics scenes uses host-authoritative state replication rather than lockstep input replication.

The boundary between authoritative state and PhysX-active simulation is the architectural seam. PhysX-active vessel state is sampled into the authoritative double-precision state representation at the sim-tick rate. The reverse direction (forces applied to PhysX-active vessels, mass updates, command injection) goes through defined sim-tick-controlled paths, not via direct PhysX mutation from arbitrary code. PhysX-active mode is a black box that the authoritative layer reads from and writes to through defined channels, never via direct access from other systems.

Vizzy scripts running on PhysX-active vessels read from the sim-tick-sampled authoritative state, not directly from PhysX. This keeps Vizzy behavior reproducible from a save — replay a save, run the same Vizzy script against the same sampled state stream, get the same script behavior. The sim tick is fixed-timestep and decoupled from rendering; PhysX FixedUpdate may run at a different rate than the sim tick; if it does, the sampling boundary is where the rates reconcile.

Authoritative state replication is the multiplayer model for PhysX-active vessels — the authoritative state sampled at each sim-tick is what the host broadcasts, and followers interpolate received state rather than running their own PhysX. Deterministic state outside the PhysX-active envelope can be replicated via lockstep input or state sync, with the specific choice deferred to multiplayer implementation. The architectural commitment is the asymmetry: PhysX-active is host-authoritative; everything outside it is deterministic and can use either model.

The gameplay-design problems of multiplayer (time-warp synchronization across players, relativistic clock synchronization, communication delay between players, etc.) are deferred to post-v1. Architecture insurance only.

### Foundational architectural principles

Five structural properties hold across the foundation resolutions above and apply project-wide as load-bearing architectural commitments. They are not optional refinements; they are the shape the foundation has, and code should be written to respect them.

**1. Single sim-tick controller as authority.** Floating-origin shifts, the analytic event-prediction priority queue, the next-event scheduling for arrivals, and the authoritative-state sampling from PhysX all live under one controller. That controller is the project's "authority attribution" scaffolding from the multiplayer-preparation requirement, instantiated for Phase 1. Every cross-cutting timing decision passes through it.

**2. Analytic events as the universal pattern for time-driven transitions.** Mode transitions, SOI re-rootings, scheduled burns, interstellar arrivals, and (eventually) orbital intersections all fit one shape: predict a time, queue it, resolve it. This is the temporal analog of the save format's "state at time T, rate function" commitment — instead of "state advances analytically," it is "transitions occur at analytically known times."

**3. Determinism boundary is the PhysX-active envelope, not the whole simulation.** Inside PhysX-active mode, simulation is non-deterministic; outside it, fully deterministic. The boundary is sharp and symmetric: PhysX-active vessel state crosses into the authoritative layer through sim-tick sampling (read side); forces, mass updates, and commands cross from the authoritative layer into PhysX-active through defined sim-tick-controlled channels (write side). These are the only legal channels across the boundary. Direct PhysX mutation from arbitrary code is not permitted. Vizzy reads authoritative state, not PhysX. Save/load reads authoritative state. Eventual multiplayer replication operates on authoritative state.

**4. Separation of simulation from presentation.** Floating-origin shifts are owned by the simulation; rendering precision is solved separately in the rendering layer. Tier 2 interstellar arrival is resolved analytically by simulation; the flyby trace is a presentation deliverable. PhysX feedback is presentation-grade physics; authoritative state is sim-grade. The simulation must be correct without any presentation layer; the presentation layer can be added or improved later without touching simulation.

**5. Reversibility named where it exists.** The floating-origin approach explicitly names vessel-local PhysX scenes as a viable refactor path with a stable external interface. The authoritative-state-sampling boundary means PhysX could in principle be replaced (with Unity Physics ECS, a custom integrator, or anything else) without disturbing the rest of the simulation. The event-type enum in the priority queue is explicitly extensible. Tier 2 arrival's stubbed Phase 1 resolution body is explicitly forward-compatible. The architecture is deliberately structured so that foundation decisions don't paint Phase 2+ into a corner.

## 3. Gameplay mechanics

### Parts and vessel construction

**LOCKED:** Procedural parts. Small base set (~30) of configurable parametric parts, infinitely tunable.

Base part categories: fuel tanks, engines (configurable power cycle, nozzle, fuel type), nose cones, decouplers, structural elements, command pods, wings/control surfaces, landing gear, RCS, science/sensor packages, antennae, solar panels, radiators, batteries, reaction wheels.

Each part has parameters that affect both visuals (geometry) and physics (mass, thrust curves, drag, structural limits, heat tolerance, operating ranges).

Vessel structure: tree (one parent per part). Symmetry tools (mirror, radial). Surface-attach plus predefined attachment nodes.

**Part categories at v1 (revised from ~30 to ~35-40):**

Parts are the universal construction vocabulary. Rockets, stations, bases, infrastructure, observatories, and propulsion systems are all built from the same parts system. Different construction contexts (ground VAB, orbital VAB, surface VAB) determine what is buildable but use the same parts vocabulary.

- Standard rocket parts (~13): pods, cabins, tanks, engines, structural, decouplers, control surfaces, legs/wheels, power, comms, sensors, heat shields, parachutes, docking ports.
- Habitats and life support (~2): habitat modules with parameters for capacity, duration, gravity context, life support level, specialization. Separate life support modules.
- Surface base modules (~6): pressurized habitats, industrial, research, power, storage, connection (corridors/airlocks), surface VAB.
- Orbital station modules (~5): pressurized modules, truss/structural, spin sections, docking hubs, orbital VAB.
- Telescopes (~1): single category with wavelength parameter (optical/infrared/UV/X-ray/radio/gravitational wave).
- Industrial modules (~4): mining, refining, manufacturing, cryogenic storage.
- Communication relays (~2): standard relays, deep-space communication infrastructure.
- Specialty propulsion infrastructure (~4): laser sail array, mass driver, refueling depot, nuclear pulse infrastructure.

**Construction contexts themselves are built.** Home ground VAB is starting infrastructure. Orbital VABs and surface VABs at other bodies are built by the player using parts they can launch or transport. Each context has scope limits (mass, dimensions, complexity) determined by what has been built.

Procedural parts produce many variants from few categories. A habitat module is not one part — it is a category with variation in capacity, duration, gravity context, life support level, specialization. 35-40 categories produces potentially hundreds of distinct part variants.

**Storage modules are universal across contexts.** A hydrogen tank is a hydrogen tank whether it's on a launch vehicle, a supply tanker, a base, or an orbital station. Water tanks, food storage, fissile fuel containers, battery banks — all use the same module categories across all construction contexts. Module capacity scales with module size (small / medium / large variants per category). Modules attach via the same docking nodes regardless of context.

**Bases and ships use the same docking standard.** Bases can dock with ships and vice versa. Module transfer is possible between docked structures (move a habitat module from a ship to a base; move a fuel tank from a base to a ship for relaunch). Some context-specific modules exist (planetary launch pad, surface VAB) that are obviously not portable, but the general parts vocabulary is interchangeable.

**Expandable habitat modules.** Some habitat modules can deploy after arrival — inflatable structures, telescoping volumes, deployed-only-after-arrival assemblies. Deployed capacity is significantly larger than transit capacity (3-5x typical). This enables habitats that are too large to launch in deployed form but feasible to launch collapsed. Trade-off: deployment is a one-way operation in most cases; once deployed, the module can't be re-collapsed for transport.

**Supplies survive rougher impact than crew.** Cargo modules can survive landings or impacts that would kill crew. Specifically: cargo modules have G-force tolerance roughly 2-3x crew tolerance. Cargo modules don't require life support during transit. Cargo modules don't require return vehicles. This is the gameplay justification for unmanned supply runs — they can use more aggressive trajectories, simpler landers, and cheaper missions than crewed equivalents.

**Procedural fuselage and wing builders.** Beyond the library-parts categories named above, two specialized procedural builders ship at Phase 5: a procedural fuselage builder and a procedural wing builder. The fuselage builder lets the player specify cross-section, length, tapering, and connection points to construct custom fuselage geometry as a single procedural part; mass, drag, and structural properties calculate from the geometry. The wing builder lets the player specify root chord, tip chord, sweep, dihedral, taper, and aerodynamic surface configuration; lift, drag, control surfaces, and structural properties calculate from the geometry. Together, the builders support serious spaceplane and atmospheric aircraft design (`### Atmospheric flight and spaceplane gameplay` in §3) where stock part libraries cannot capture the design space.

**Parts visual progression across phases.** The parts system develops across the phases with specific scope per phase. Phase 1-3 ships the simplest library-parts set sufficient for testing the flight model, vehicle assembly, and the validation milestones (placeholder cube to orbit in Phase 1; built rocket to Mun landing in Phase 3). Phase 4 adds procgen variation per part category — each part type has visual variants with consistent functional properties, giving vessel designs visual identity without changing the parts vocabulary. Phase 5 adds the procedural fuselage and wing builders (named above) plus any remaining specialized builders. The phase progression keeps Phases 1-3 focused on functional correctness and defers visual richness to Phase 4 and procedural authoring to Phase 5, consistent with the design strategy commitment in §1.

**Post-v1.1 part-system features.** Two ambitious parts-system features are deliberately deferred to v1.1 (post-launch): painting and texturing of parts and built vessels with player-controlled visual treatment (procedural texturing, decals, color schemes, weathering); and ambitious mesh-composition features (constructing custom-shaped parts from primitive operations, more elaborate procedural builders for other categories beyond fuselage and wings). These are visual-polish features that the v1 parts system supports architecturally but does not ship at v1 launch. Deferring them protects the v1 ship date; keeping them architecturally supportable protects post-launch capability expansion.

### Failure modes

**DEFAULT:** Selective failure modes that create engineering decisions. Player-configurable difficulty.

Failure modes that create decisions (not tedium):

- Engine ignition failure probability (build in redundancy)
- Component temperature/stress limits (design within margins)
- Fuel boil-off over long missions (insulation, mission planning)
- Solar panel degradation from radiation (overdesign or replace)
- Communication delay scaling with distance (mission planning)
- Crew life support consumption (resupply or design for autonomy)

All scaled by player difficulty settings (off / cosmetic / functional / brutal). Defaults are friendly for new players.

**Ship and base failure modes share vocabulary.** Bases and ships are built from the same parts (commit 011a) and therefore have similar failure modes. Non-critical instrument failures (telescope module failed, mass spectrometer module failed, communications array degraded) lose specific functionality without disabling the whole structure. Life support failures on bases manifest the same way as on ships — radiation exposure, decompression, supply depletion, contamination — with the same player response options.

This shared vocabulary means players who learn ship failure handling already know base failure handling. The mental model transfers.

### Sensors as vessel components

**LOCKED:** Vessels carry sensors. Sensor suite affects what the vessel can detect during flyby, orbital, surface operations. This connects vessel design to mission outcomes — what you can learn at the target depends on what you brought.

**Sensor categories:**

- **Cameras** (visible, multi-spectral, hyperspectral) — for imaging at varying resolution and wavelength coverage.
- **Spectrometers** (UV, visible, IR) — for chemistry, atmospheric composition, surface mineralogy.
- **Magnetometers** — for magnetic field measurement during flyby or orbital phases.
- **Radar** — for surface mapping, altimetry, subsurface investigation.
- **Mass spectrometers** — for direct atmospheric or surface chemistry sampling.
- **Particle detectors** — for cosmic rays, solar wind, energetic particles, radiation environment.
- **Seismometers** — surface-deployed instruments for geological measurement.
- **Sample-return systems** — for direct sampling with return to home for laboratory analysis.

Each sensor adds mass and complexity to the vessel. Each unlocks specific observation capabilities at the target. Mission planning becomes "what do I need to learn at this target, and what sensors do I need to learn it?"

Sensors are part categories in the parts vocabulary (commit 011a). Procedural part variation produces specific sensor instances from each category (different sensitivity, mass, power requirements).

### Automation and scripting

**LOCKED:** Vizzy-style block-based visual scripting.

Rationale: discoverable for newcomers, deep enough for power users (Juno players have built full guidance computers in Vizzy). Implementation cost comparable to text scripting. Investigate xNode or Unity's Visual Scripting as foundation.

Implementation notes:

- Scripts persist with vessel designs and saved games
- Scripts run in a sandboxed VM, deterministic execution (multiplayer requirement)
- Clean export/import format for sharing (compensates for the text-scripting share-friendliness gap)
- Automation extends to supply chains, not just vessel control — set up an automated tanker route once, it runs on its own

**Vizzy scripts do not run during time-warp on Kepler-rails vessels.** Scripts sleep when their vessel enters Kepler-rails mode; they wake when the vessel returns to PhysX-active. This is what makes Vizzy compatible with the determinism boundary from section 2: scripts read sim-tick-sampled authoritative state during PhysX-active flight, which is well-defined; they would have no well-defined read source during high-warp Kepler-rails propagation, where there is no sim-tick sampling of vessel state because the vessel state is being advanced analytically. Vizzy is a flight automation tool, not a monitoring tool.

**Monitoring needs are served by the alert system.** The alert system is a first-class foundational system, not a UI element. Alerts watch for conditions ("fuel below 10%," "periapsis below 70 km," "vessel within 1000 km of target," "communication lost for more than an hour") and notify the player when triggered. Alerts run at warp scale and operate against the same analytic state the priority queue from section 2 operates on. Vizzy scripts can configure alerts as part of their setup — a script runs once during launch to register the alerts it needs, then sleeps when the vessel enters Kepler-rails; the alert system continues watching at warp scale and surfaces notifications when triggered.

The alert system and the analytic event-prediction priority queue from section 2 both predict when conditions become true and fire events. They may share infrastructure when both are built in Phase 5; the specific architectural decision (shared or separate) defers to that phase. The systems should be designed with awareness of each other.

**Mobile shipping note.** Vizzy is the part of the game least suited to mobile. Node graph editors are notoriously painful on small touchscreens — dense graphs, fat-finger imprecision, small affordances. If mobile shipping happens (see section 10's `Mobile shipping` open question), the expected pattern is a read-only Vizzy viewer on mobile plus full desktop authoring rather than a full mobile authoring experience. Architecturally cheap if known now; expensive to retrofit later. The architecture commitment is that Vizzy scripts persist with vessel designs (already locked) and can be loaded and executed on any platform; only the authoring UI is platform-restricted. Mobile users can run desktop-authored scripts; they cannot author new ones from mobile.

### Flight computers and unmanned operation

**LOCKED:** A flight computer is a discrete part category enabling unmanned vessel operation. The flight computer is not artificial intelligence in any deep sense — it is a guidance and automation system roughly equivalent to the autopilot computer in current spacecraft, capable of executing pre-planned procedures and pre-flown routes but not improvising novel solutions.

**Flight computer as a discrete part.** Vessels with a flight computer installed can operate unmanned. Vessels without one require crew to operate beyond minimum stability. This is a deliberate design choice — the flight computer is a real part with mass, cost, and design implications, not an invisible universal capability.

**Manned vessels without flight computer.** A manned vessel without a flight computer can fly. Basic stability (auto-hold-attitude) is always available — the vessel's reaction wheels and thrusters can maintain orientation without a flight computer module. But advanced maneuvers (the nine primitives listed below) require either a flight computer module or direct crew control. Crew can always manually fly anything; advanced automation requires the part.

This produces meaningful build decisions. A small crewed probe might omit the flight computer to save mass — the crew handles maneuvers manually. A large station's flight computer is essential because hand-flying a large vessel through complex maneuvers is impractical. An unmanned probe requires the flight computer absolutely (no crew to fall back on).

**What flight computers enable:**

- **Unmanned launches and missions:** Probes, satellites, supply craft can fly without crew. Unmanned craft accept G-force loads that would kill humans, simpler life support, no return vehicle requirement (one-way missions feasible).
- **Pre-flown route execution:** Routes the player has flown successfully can be executed autonomously by a flight computer thereafter. Supply routes (`### Supply lines` in §5) typically run on this model after their first manual run.
- **Mapping assistance:** Flight computers on probes can scan and report autonomously, contributing to the catalog without requiring crew attention.
- **Long-duration cruise:** Interstellar incubation missions (below) use flight computers as their sole guidance system over decades or centuries of cruise.
- **Simulator integration:** Mission Control's simulator (`### Sandbox-as-simulator integrated in Mission Control` in §7) uses flight computer logic for sim-controlled craft.

**Manned vs unmanned trade-offs:**

| Aspect | Manned | Unmanned |
|--------|--------|----------|
| G-force tolerance | Crew-tolerance (varies per person) | 2-3x crew tolerance |
| Life support | Required, mass cost | None |
| Return vehicle | Usually required | Optional (one-way feasible) |
| Adaptability | Improvisation possible | Pre-planned procedures only |
| Mission duration | Bounded by life support and crew endurance | Bounded by hardware degradation only |
| Cost | Higher (life support, return capability) | Lower |
| Risk weight | Crew lives | Hardware cost |

Players choose manned vs unmanned per mission based on what's needed. Discovery missions to nearby targets often manned (improvisation valuable). Supply routes often unmanned (pre-planned, repetitive). Long-duration interstellar missions usually unmanned (life support over decades is intractable).

**Advanced maneuvering primitives.** Flight computers support a vocabulary of automated maneuvers that Vizzy scripts (`### Automation and scripting` in §3) can invoke:

- Match velocity with target vessel
- Match orbit with target body or vessel
- Approach to specified range
- Station-keeping at specified relative position
- Match rotation with target
- Dock at specified port
- Hold attitude (specified orientation)
- Burn to target orbital parameters
- Hold altitude (atmospheric flight)

These are primitives — building blocks Vizzy scripts compose into complex behavior.

**Incubation modules.** A specialized form of unmanned mission: a colony seed package combined with flight computer guidance, accelerated to high velocity by laser sail (`### Interstellar travel: tiered tech progression` in §4), cruising for decades or centuries to a target star system. On arrival, the incubation module attempts to deploy colony infrastructure autonomously — assemble habitats, deploy power systems, begin resource extraction, await later crewed missions.

**Success model.** Lower chance of success than crewed missions because no improvisation is possible. Two layers govern success:

- **Failure-mode-tied probabilities the player affects through engineering.** Specific failure modes scale with specific engineering decisions:
    - Signal loss during cruise — probability scales with comms quality (redundant transmitters, signal power, error correction systems).
    - Navigation drift — probability scales with cruise duration and mid-course correction system reliability.
    - Deceleration system failure — probability scales with system redundancy margin (backup engines, fuel reserve).
    - Autonomous deployment errors — probability scales with deployment system complexity and pre-flight testing.
- **Fixed per-design success multiplier.** The incubator module itself has a baseline success multiplier that modifies all failure-mode probabilities. This is a parameter of the incubator design, not a player-tunable quality dial (the game has no monetary system to give "quality" gameplay weight). The multiplier varies modestly across incubator design generations as the player's program develops better incubator technology through research.

Both layers compose: an engineering-attentive player who builds redundant comms, ample fuel reserves, and conservative deployment systems gets significantly better odds than one who builds minimum-mass incubators. The fixed multiplier provides the baseline; engineering decisions adjust around it.

Incubation is the cheapest interstellar-tier mission: no crew, no life support during cruise, no return vehicle. But it commits the player to the destination for decades before knowing if it worked.

### Atmospheric flight and spaceplane gameplay

**LOCKED:** Atmospheric flight is a first-class engineering discipline, not a launch-phase detail. Spaceplanes are a real vessel category with their own design problem, their own gameplay loop, and their own mission types. Aircraft design for high-atmosphere science platforms, sample-return on bodies with atmospheres, and SSTO/spaceplane ascent profiles are all supported.

**Aerodynamic fidelity.** Aerodynamics are simulated at Juno: New Origins fidelity — procedural aero surfaces with lift/drag/moment calculated from surface geometry, angle of attack, and local airflow conditions. This is the resolution of the Phase 3 aerodynamic model question from §10 (raycast-based was the middle-ground default; the locked choice is Juno-style procedural aero surfaces, which is the upper end of that bracket). The model is honest enough that real aerodynamic concerns matter — center of lift relative to center of mass, stall behavior, control authority across the speed envelope, drag minimization as a real engineering objective.

**Flight regimes.** Subsonic, transonic, supersonic, and hypersonic regimes are all in scope. Each regime has characteristic aerodynamic behavior — shock formation at transonic boundary, wave drag dominance in supersonic, thermal management dominance in hypersonic. The model reproduces qualitative phenomena (drag rise through transonic, shock waves visible in flight effects, sonic boom audio in atmospheric flight near surface) rather than research-grade quantitative fidelity.

**Re-entry heating.** Thermal modeling during atmospheric re-entry is in scope. Heating rate depends on velocity, atmospheric density, and vehicle geometry (blunt-body vs sharp). Heat shields are real parts with thermal capacity, ablation behavior, and failure modes. Re-entry corridor width depends on vehicle thermal budget and approach angle — too steep and the craft burns; too shallow and it skips off the atmosphere. Re-entry plasma visuals (`### Phase 4 — Visuals` in §9) match the underlying thermal model.

**Spaceplane gameplay.** Spaceplanes are vessels that combine aerodynamic lift and rocket propulsion. They take off from runways, climb through the atmosphere as aircraft, transition to rocket power at altitude, and reach orbit on combined airbreathing-plus-rocket ascent profiles. Returning spaceplanes glide through re-entry and land on runways. Spaceplane missions trade launch-mass-efficiency (SSTO with full payload return capability) against engineering difficulty (the design problem is harder than vertical-launch rockets). Players who solve the engineering get reusability; players who don't lose mass margin to less optimal architectures.

**Atmospheric science platforms.** Aircraft designed for high-altitude or long-duration atmospheric flight serve scientific missions — sustained observation of a body's atmosphere, atmospheric sample collection, weather monitoring, surface mapping at low altitude. These are aircraft, not rockets — propellers, jets, or solar-electric depending on the body's atmospheric composition and stellar flux. Atmospheric science platforms are the natural use case for bodies with thick atmospheres and interesting weather (`### Feature-layer architecture` and `### Asteroid clusters as system composition variant` in §6 establish extreme-weather feature layers that atmospheric platforms can investigate).

**Cross-references to vessel construction and parts.** Atmospheric flight requires the procedural fuselage and wing builders specified in `### Parts and vessel construction` (§3). The library-parts progression from Phase 1-3 is sufficient for testing the flight model; Phase 4 procgen variation produces visually distinct part variants; Phase 5 procedural builders enable full custom geometry for serious spaceplane design. Layer 1 players (commit 006) use defaults or template aircraft; Layer 4 players (`### Layered engagement framework` in §8) optimize wing geometry, transition profiles, and re-entry corridors for specific mission requirements.

**Atmospheric flight at non-home bodies.** Each body with an atmosphere supports atmospheric flight per its physical parameters — atmospheric density profile, gravity, stellar flux for solar-electric, available combustion oxidizer (atmospheric oxygen on Earth-equivalents; alternative chemistries on other compositions). A propeller aircraft on a thick-atmosphere Venus-equivalent flies differently than a jet on an Earth-equivalent. The physics is honest across bodies; the engineering is the player's responsibility.

### Crew and characters

**LOCKED:** A single stylized humanoid species. Crew tracked as headcount for base mechanics, as named individuals for important missions. Mortality on by default, toggleable in difficulty settings.

Characters serve three functions, in order of importance:

- **Stakes.** A vehicle moving to a location is not the same as someone going somewhere. Characters make achievements feel like achievements and losses feel like losses.
- **Scale.** Tiny figures in cockpits, on surfaces, in habitat windows ground the size of everything around them.
- **Emotional payload for time-dilation.** The single most novel mechanic in the game (named crew arriving home decades after their families have aged) requires characters to land emotionally.

Design principles:

- Stylization register matches physics register. Game uses scaled-down semi-stylized physics; characters use the same register. Mid-stylization humanoids — distinctive, expressive, not photoreal. Single species for v1.
- Earnest, not wacky. Kerbal humor works because Kerbals are sincere characters in absurd situations. Designed comedy ages poorly; situational comedy from earnest characters does not. Pixar register, not Goat Simulator register.
- Procedural names, minimal traits. Generated names, optional one-line specialty (engineer / pilot / scientist). No deep written backstories. Player imagination fills in the rest — this is how Jeb became a legend.
- Visually present, not the focus. Visible in cockpits, on EVA, planting flags, in base habitat views. Not the main attraction. Juno-style presence.
- Mortality matters. Crew can die. This is the single biggest emotional lever. Toggleable via difficulty settings for players who don't want permadeath.
- Time-dilation is the unique hook. A named crew of four leaves on a 40-game-year colony voyage. The home system ages while they don't. When they arrive, the player feels what was paid for the journey. No other space sim has this. Use it.

Implementation cost is small: a name, a portrait or simple model variant, a status (alive / lost / aged / in-transit / in-cryo), and the difficulty-toggle hooks. Most of the work is visual presence — making sure characters appear in the right places at the right scale. Names and portraits are procedurally generated.

**Crew are physically located on vessels.** Each crew member is at a specific location at any given time — aboard a specific vessel, at a specific base, or in transit. To move a crew member between locations, you launch a vessel that carries them. Crew rotation is a real logistics problem: shifting a crew member from the home base to Mars requires a vessel going there with capacity for them; returning them requires a return vessel; rotating long-duration crews requires scheduled crew-transport routes.

This makes crew rotation an engineering decision rather than a UI abstraction. A crewed mission that fails strands the crew. A crewed vessel needs life support for everyone aboard. Crew assignment to a long mission means those individuals are unavailable for other missions until they return. The dramatic shapes (commit 012) that involve crew — the rescue, the disaster, the cost — emerge from the physicality.

**Crew as a finite resource.** The player's program has finite crew. Each crew member is one person with their own training, history, and skills. Crew are not interchangeable. Sending crew on a long mission means those specific individuals are unavailable for other missions until they return — or, in the case of interstellar missions, possibly never. The player's choices about who goes where shape the program's character.

**Crew tolerances and endurance growth.** Each crew member has individual tolerances for G-force, radiation, zero-G exposure, and mission stress. Tolerances vary across crew — some are tougher than others.

Crew accumulate endurance through total flight time, with three specific commitments governing the growth:

- **Per-category growth.** Endurance growth is tracked separately per exposure category. A crew member who has flown many high-G missions builds G-force tolerance specifically; the same crew member's radiation tolerance does not grow unless they've been exposed to radiation. Each category accumulates independently based on actual exposure during flight time.
- **Logarithmic curve.** Growth follows diminishing returns. Rapid improvement in the first hundreds of hours of flight time within a category; slow improvement after thousands. A rookie improves visibly with their first few missions; a veteran's gains are marginal per mission.
- **Soft ceiling at ~2-3x rookie tolerance.** No crew member becomes effectively invincible. The veteran ceiling sits at roughly 2-3x the rookie baseline. Specific magnitudes (rookie baseline values, ceiling multipliers per category) are balance parameters to be tuned during Phase 5.

This creates real attachment to specific crew. A veteran isn't just a name — they're the only person on the program who can safely fly the high-G missions because they're the only person with G-force tolerance that has actually grown. Losing them means losing capability that takes game-years to rebuild.

### EVA as temporary character control

**LOCKED:** EVA exists as a contextual control mode. When EVA is needed (planting a flag, surface exploration, an Armstrong moment), the director temporarily takes direct control of a named crew member. First or third-person view. The character is physically simulated in PhysX-active mode (subject to gravity, atmosphere, terrain collision).

EVA outcomes are non-deterministic in the same way other PhysX-active outcomes are non-deterministic per section 2's `### Foundational architectural principles` — this is fine because EVA is per-player exploration content, not authoritative shared state. EVA-state changes that matter (samples collected, flag planted, science data recorded) are sampled into authoritative state at sim-tick boundaries the same way vessel state is.

Returns to vessel-level director control when EVA ends.

### Director perspective

**LOCKED:** The player operates at vessel/agency level. They direct missions, manage bases, plan operations. The player exists outside the game world as the director of the agency.

Crew on vessels are named characters with their own proper-time clocks (in single-player, these can diverge from game-time during relativistic transit), relationships, ages, and mortality. The player witnesses time-dilation rather than experiencing it first-person — in single-player, the director sees a colony ship leave with a 30-year-old crew, watches 50 game-years pass at home, welcomes back a 50-year-old crew while their families have aged 80. The emotional weight is real and operates through the director's investment in their crew and program, not through first-person time-dilation.

This is the perspective of mission control, not the astronaut — which is the perspective of every space program in human history. Apollo 13's drama was experienced by mission control as much as by the astronauts.

This subsection establishes the player's primary perspective. The next subsection (`### Crew and characters`) describes who the crew are; the EVA subsection after it describes the one moment when the player temporarily takes direct control.

### Mission planning as structured persistent activity

**LOCKED:** Missions are the persistent unit of player work. A mission is a named, planned, multi-stage activity with objectives, vessels assigned, crew assigned, supply requirements identified, success criteria defined, and a transmission log. Missions persist across game-time on the scale of weeks to decades.

**Mission lifecycle states:** planning, launched, in transit, on station, returning, complete, failed.

**What missions provide structurally:**

- Vessels are mission-assigned (a vessel is doing something specific, not free-floating)
- Crew are mission-assigned (named individuals are on specific work)
- Supplies feed missions (logistics is per-mission, not abstract)
- Observations are mission outputs (catalog entries trace back to specific missions)
- Anomalies trigger new missions (discovery and investigation are mission-shaped)
- Transmissions are mission-tagged (radio chatter and event logs have context)

The mission UI is where the player tracks ongoing work. Late-game players have dozens of concurrent missions in various states. The catalog becomes "what missions have produced." The dramatic shapes are mission-shaped (the rescue is a mission to recover; the arrival is a mission reaching its destination; the disaster is a mission gone wrong).

This is structural overlay, not new mechanics. Missions organize existing systems so the player stays oriented in late-game complexity.

**Satellite and infrastructure repair missions.** Some missions are repair operations rather than discovery or construction. Inherited or player-built infrastructure occasionally fails — a satellite's instrument module degrades, a base's life support module malfunctions, a deep-space probe loses comms. Repair missions involve dispatching crew or a flight-computer-equipped repair craft to the failure location with replacement modules or supplies. Successful repair restores the asset; failed repair (insufficient supplies, inappropriate replacement parts, crew loss en route) leaves the asset offline pending another attempt.

Satellite repair is a frequent mission type because satellites accumulate failures over game-years. Players who maintain a substantial orbital infrastructure manage an ongoing repair schedule. This is part of the program's operational reality, not a special event.

### Campaigns as multi-mission programs

**LOCKED:** A campaign is a named long-running goal with multiple missions feeding it. The Mars colonization campaign. The interstellar reconnaissance campaign. The home observatory expansion campaign. Campaigns can span decades of game-time and dozens of missions.

**Campaign state includes:** name, declared goal, missions belonging to the campaign (planned / in progress / complete / failed), accumulated progress toward the goal, current bottleneck, key metrics specific to the campaign.

The campaign UI sits one level above the mission UI. The player sees at a glance what each major effort has accomplished: "Mars colonization: 12 missions complete, 3 in progress, infrastructure 40% complete, current bottleneck = fuel production capacity."

Campaigns are how the player engages with the game at the right scale for late-game play. Without campaigns, dozens of concurrent missions become unmanageable. With campaigns, the player has a coherent view of long-arc work.

This is structural overlay over missions. No new mechanics required — campaigns are containers for related missions.

### Engineering documentation produced by play

**LOCKED:** Every vessel design accumulates a history. Each design records: total launches, success rate, failure modes encountered, modifications made, missions served on, performance metrics across launches (delta-v actually achieved vs predicted, payload mass delivered, mission duration vs planned).

A successful design ("Heavy Lifter Mk IV, 47 successful launches, average payload mass 12.3 tons, two recoverable failures both in atmospheric phase") becomes a tool the player trusts. A new design feels new because there's no track record. Players who iterate on a design see the history accumulate across iterations.

This is data attached to the design (not the individual vessel instance). The design is mode-portable (commit 004a); the history is portable with it. Players sharing designs can include or strip the history when sharing.

This connects to: dramatic shapes (the long-trusted design that finally fails has weight because of the accumulated history), engineering as the verb (real engineering produces real records), the catalog (designs are searchable alongside discoveries).

### Failure forensics

**LOCKED:** When a vessel fails, the player can investigate. Every vessel records telemetry continuously throughout its operation. Failure produces a post-incident review showing telemetry leading up to the event, identification of the proximate cause (engine overpressure at T+45s, thermal limit exceeded, fuel exhaustion, structural failure), and where applicable the design or operational decisions that contributed.

The investigation is a player verb, not an automatic notification. The player can choose to review carefully (catching subtle issues, learning the design's weaknesses) or accept the summary and move on. Layer 4 players (commit 006) get the raw telemetry; Layer 1 players get "engine failed due to overpressure" without needing to interpret it themselves.

This connects real telemetry (commit 011a) and procedural failure modes (commit 011a) into a player-facing loop. Failures become learning opportunities rather than punishments. The dramatic shape "the disaster" gains forensic depth — the player understands what went wrong.

### Mode-portable designs and templates

**LOCKED:** Vessel designs (parts, configuration, Vizzy scripts) and character templates (names, traits, portraits) are mode-portable. Players can import designs and templates created in single-player into multiplayer and vice versa. Only runtime state (vessel instances in the simulation, character instances with their accumulated history, save state with diverged proper-time) is mode-locked.

The distinction: designs and templates describe how to construct; runtime state describes what currently exists.

**Format requirements:**

- Self-contained (no external references, no save-game dependencies).
- Explicit semantic versioning of the format itself, independent of game version. Designs created in any version load in any later version with graceful degradation when parts have changed or been removed.
- Human-readable enough to enable community tooling (visualizers, validators, batch operations).
- Exportable and importable as files or codes.

**Vizzy scripts in shared designs use role-based references by default.** Parts are referenced by role (e.g., "main engine," "primary tank") rather than by ID, with optional ID overrides for advanced cases. This is what makes scripts portable across shared designs — a script that says "fire the main engine" works in any design that has a part marked with that role.

**Community sharing infrastructure is not v1 but the format must support it.** First-party online sharing (a website where players post and download designs) is not a v1 deliverable. The export/import format must support such infrastructure being added later. For v1, players share designs as files or codes through whatever third-party channels they prefer (Discord, forums, Reddit).

**Local-first, offline forever.** Design and template file usage is purely local — no online requirement, no account system, no DRM. Sharing infrastructure (if and when built) is additive. Designs and templates work offline forever: no phone-home, no license check, no expiring assets. Players who buy the game in year N can still load their year-N designs in year N+15.

### Movie-moment mechanics

**LOCKED:** Six mechanics produce the engineering-improvisation moments characteristic of the genre's most beloved scenes (Interstellar docking with spinning Endurance, Gravity using landing fuel for attitude, Martian counter-thrust against storm). The shared property: multi-purpose physical systems that can be used in ways the designer did not anticipate.

**Differential thrust from multi-engine clusters.** Individual engine throttle control including shutting some down. Asymmetric thrust for attitude or counter-force.

**Thrust vectoring beyond designed range.** Player can override gimbal limits at risk of mechanical damage. Engines can be pushed past their rated parameters in emergencies; stress accumulates.

**Spin gravity through actual rotation.** Build a craft that spins. Crew on outer ring experience artificial gravity. Real physics including Coriolis force in spinning frames, structural stress, momentum management.

**Communication delay with real consequences.** Real light-speed delay. Distant missions require autonomy. Vizzy scripting becomes essential beyond reasonable command-and-control delay.

**Procedural failure modes.** Mechanical failures are varied and probabilistic. Not just "engine explodes." Flame-outs, ignition failures, overheating, fuel leaks, turbopump failure, thrust vector control loss, combustion instability.

**Real telemetry revealing problems.** Sensors detect real conditions. Temperature rises before engine fails. Voltage drops before battery dies. Players who watch can catch problems early.

The implementation discipline: make the physics real and legible. Do not add gameplay layers on top of physics. No "docking skill check," no "engineering bonus." The physics is the gameplay. Players who understand physics can do interesting things.

### Dual-use technology framework

**LOCKED:** Destruction emerges from physics, not from dedicated combat systems. Tools that exist for legitimate engineering purposes have predictable destructive consequences when applied to fragile targets. The destructive use is consequence-of-physics, not weapons-category.

The discipline: tech tree has terraforming tools, propulsion infrastructure, energy systems. No "weapons" tab. Mission planning has "deliver payload to body" with payload type "nuclear device," not "target enemy ship" with targeting reticle.

**Dual-use technologies:**

- **Nuclear devices.** Terraforming (melt ice caps), asteroid disruption, excavation, Project Orion pulse propulsion, destruction of fragile targets.
- **High-power lasers.** Sail propulsion, power transmission to distant craft, communications, sensor disruption, component damage, atmospheric heating, surface vaporization.
- **Mass drivers.** Surface-to-orbit launch, cargo delivery between bodies, asteroid redirection, kinetic impactors, debris clearance.
- **Reactor technology.** Base power, vessel propulsion (nuclear thermal/electric/pulse), industrial heat, controlled criticality, accidental excursion.
- **Plasma and ion technologies.** Ion drives, plasma propulsion, magnetic shielding (same coils), industrial plasma processing, magnetic sail propulsion from stellar wind.

**Server rules govern destructive use against other agencies.** Cooperative servers protect other agencies' assets. Competitive servers permit physical-consequence engagement. Self-destruction is unrestricted (player can nuke their own base for terraforming purposes).

**Friction comes from physics.** Nuking a base requires getting the device there. Frying electronics with a laser requires the target in range with sufficient power. Real engineering problems, not click-to-damage interactions.

**Endgame propulsion tiers:** Chemical (starting), nuclear thermal, laser sail one-way (tier 2 interstellar), laser sail two-way (tier 3 interstellar, requires destination infrastructure), fusion (helium-3 fueled, advanced), antimatter (highest, post-v1 or very late). No FTL beyond physics-grounded options. Star Trek warp would undermine the time-dilation pillar and is rejected.

### Goal structure

**DEFAULT:** Sandbox-first with optional purpose. No mission grind, no KSP-style career mode.

Purpose comes from: anomalies detected by observatories (go investigate), tech tree unlocks (build to reach the next capability), resource needs (mine, refine, transport), exploration milestones (first to reach X), emergent goals from time-dilation (do something before your home colony's situation changes), and crew-driven emotional stakes (your veteran pilot is on the colony ship; will she make it home before her sister dies of old age?).

### Investment depth as graduated game response

**LOCKED:** The game can detect player investment depth through observable signals (named crew, bases built at home, time in home system, transmissions monitored vs accumulated, crew biographies read or skipped) and modulate the texture of dilation experiences accordingly without changing gameplay mechanics.

A player with high investment signals gets more cinematic arrival moments — more transmissions, more crew interaction, more home colony state changes surfaced. A player with low investment signals gets the same mechanical outcome (mission returns, time has advanced) without the emotional production.

This is not manipulation. It is honest service of different play styles. The engineer gets clean mechanics. The narrator gets full dramatic payoff. Same game, different surfacing based on what the player has signaled they care about. Respects that different players want different experiences.

## 4. World behavior and discovery

### Information asymmetry / progressive discovery

**LOCKED:** Knowledge about a celestial body is acquired in tiers, not granted by default.

- From home: spectroscopic data, rough mass, rough orbit. A point of light with metadata.
- Flyby: rough imaging, magnetic field, gravity field detail.
- Orbit: surface maps, atmosphere composition, weather patterns, detailed resource surveys.
- Surface: physical samples, ground truth, full local data, viable mining sites identified.

Implication for data model: each body has 'true' state plus per-agency 'observed' state, accumulated by the agency's instruments and missions. UI shows the active agency's observed state until upgraded. Resource availability is revealed progressively through observation tiers — you can't mine what you don't know is there.

**Agency-based observation sharing.** Observation state is per-agency, not per-player. An "agency" is the player-facing organization the player is running — what does the work and accumulates the knowledge. Each agency has its own observation tier per body and its own catalog of discoveries.

Agency membership refers to human players. Crew on missions are employed by the agency, not members of it: a crew member is a named individual the agency hires and assigns; an agency member is a human player who participates in directing the agency. In single-player, the agency has exactly one human member (the player) plus however many crew the player has assigned. In multiplayer, an agency may have multiple human members; multiple agencies may exist in one multiplayer session with their own memberships.

Cross-agency observation sharing has three modes the multiplayer architecture supports, with the default for any given mode decided when multiplayer ships post-v1:

- **Within an agency, sharing is instant.** All human members of one agency see the same observations as soon as they are made.
- **Across agencies, sharing routes through comms.** Agency A broadcasts a discovery; agency B receives it after the appropriate comms delay (light-speed for interstellar broadcasts, scaled or instant per difficulty settings for in-system).
- **In competitive games, cross-agency data may be hidden entirely.** Agency A's observations are private to agency A; other agencies must discover independently.

This architecturally supports: shared knowledge (everyone in one agency), asymmetric with instant sharing (each player their own agency with permissive rules), asymmetric with comms delay (each player their own agency with realistic comms), and hybrid team-based play (multiple multi-player agencies). The data model has agencies even when there is one human player; single-player is the trivial reduction where the agency has one member.

**Ground-truth verification.** When a vessel reaches a body that's been observed remotely, the differences between remote observation and direct measurement are surfaced. Remote spectroscopy estimated 80% nitrogen / 20% oxygen; direct atmospheric sampling shows 78% nitrogen / 19% oxygen / 3% argon. Most differences are small (remote observation is mostly accurate). Some are significant (a biosignature confirmed or disconfirmed; a resource estimate revised by 30%; an anomaly resolved). The differences are gameplay — they reveal the limits of remote observation and reward direct investigation.

### Detection ecosystem

**LOCKED:** Discovery operates through a suite of distinct detection methods, each with its own physical character, its own platform requirements, its own gameplay role, and its own characteristic blind spots. No single method reveals everything. Depth comes from combination. Mystery is preserved by the limits of each method, not by arbitrary gating.

This extends and operationalizes the observation tier system in `### Information asymmetry / progressive discovery`. The four observation tiers (home / flyby / orbit / surface) remain valid as coarse categories. The detection methods and platforms below are the multi-instrument approach within each tier.

**Seven detection method categories:**

**Visible light astronomy.** Photometry, imaging, color information, spectroscopy. Available from ground or space. Misses cold objects, dust-obscured objects, objects emitting in non-visible wavelengths.

**Infrared astronomy.** Heat. Cold objects, dust clouds, planets cooler than their stars, internal heat, planetary thermal emission, dust-shrouded star formation. Space-based instruments outperform ground; better with cold detectors.

**Ultraviolet astronomy.** Hot phenomena and active chemistry. Young stars, stellar flares, certain atmospheric markers. Requires detectors above atmosphere — UV is absorbed.

**X-ray astronomy.** Neutron stars, black hole accretion, hot gas, stellar coronae, magnetar emission. Requires orbital observatories — X-rays don't penetrate atmosphere.

**Gamma-ray astronomy.** Gamma-ray bursts, pulsar wind nebulae, certain supernovae. Often transient. Requires orbital instruments.

**Radio astronomy.** Pulsars, masers, hydrogen 21cm (interstellar gas mapping), synchrotron radiation, redshift, planetary radio emission (gas giants are loud), atmospheric chemistry signatures, narrowband SETI-style signals. Reveals a nearly orthogonal set of objects from optical. Ground-based works; larger antennas resolve finer details. Lunar far-side radio is particularly valuable because it shields from home-planet radio noise.

**Particle detection and gravitational wave detection.** Neutrinos from stellar cores and supernovae. Cosmic rays from acceleration in stellar remnants and galactic nuclei. Gravitational wave detection for inspiraling compact objects. Reveals processes invisible to electromagnetic radiation. Requires specialized detectors; often deep-space or deep-underground.

**Direct sampling.** Going to a place and analyzing it directly. Atmospheric probes, sample return, in-situ instruments. Ground truth. Requires physical presence.

### Platforms for detection

Detection methods are deployed on platforms. Each platform has its own gameplay role.

**Ground-based observatories.** Cheap to build, can be very large (no launch mass limit), maintainable. Limited by atmospheric absorption (no UV, X-ray, gamma-ray; limited infrared), atmospheric turbulence, weather, daylight, sky visibility. Home planet starts with ground observatories. Location matters — high altitude is better, equatorial gives more sky access, polar gives long-duration single-target observations.

**Space-based observatories.** Above atmosphere. Access to all wavelengths, no weather, can be deeply cooled for infrared. More expensive (launch costs), harder to maintain. Major mid-game capability. Hubble-equivalent or JWST-equivalent unlocks observation modes ground telescopes can't do. Servicing missions are their own gameplay.

**Lunar / moon-surface observatories.** No atmosphere, very stable platform, far-side shields from home-planet radio noise. Major investment but unique capabilities. High-tier endgame.

**Interferometric arrays.** Multiple instruments combined to act as one giant instrument. Resolution improves with baseline distance. Two observatories on opposite sides of the home planet are an interferometer. Adding space-based and lunar instruments extends the baseline.

**Mobile platforms.** Flyby probes, orbiting satellites at other bodies, rovers, atmospheric probes, balloons. Each gives different access. Mission planning chooses platform for context.

**Ground-based vehicles on other bodies.** Rovers, drills, atmospheric processors. Site selection matters; mobility matters.

**Wavelength-specific atmospheric limits for ground observatories.** The home planet's atmosphere imposes specific limits on what ground telescopes can detect. Visible light passes well at most times (limited by atmospheric turbulence and weather). Near-infrared works but degrades through atmospheric water vapor absorption. Mid- and far-infrared require above-atmosphere observation or dry high-altitude sites. Ultraviolet is largely absorbed by ozone — ground UV astronomy is impossible. X-ray and gamma-ray are fully absorbed by the atmosphere — require orbital instruments. Radio mostly passes (different frequency bands have different ionospheric and water-vapor effects).

These atmospheric limits make space-based observatories qualitatively more capable, not just larger — they open observation modes ground telescopes can't access at any size. The progression from ground-based to space-based observatory infrastructure is gated by wavelength capability, not just resolution.

### Discovery progression — eight stages

The four observation tiers from `### Information asymmetry / progressive discovery` are coarse categories. Within and across them, discovery proceeds through eight fine-grained stages. A given body might be at Stage 3 while still in the "home" tier — it depends on what instruments have been pointed at it.

**Stage 0: Total ignorance.** Body exists in procgen but player has no idea. One of many unobserved bodies.

**Stage 1: Coarse detection.** Some instrument picks up something. Slight gravitational deviation, periodic dimming (transit), weak radio signature. First detection is anomalous data. Body enters the catalog as a candidate.

**Stage 2: Confirmation and basic characterization.** Multiple detection methods confirm the same object. Independent measurements increase confidence. Mass estimate from radial velocity. Radius estimate from transit photometry. Combined gives density, which gives composition.

**Stage 3: Spectroscopic atmosphere.** As techniques improve, atmospheric data. Transit spectroscopy, thermal emission, reflection spectrum. Composition revealed. Oxygen at significant levels is a biosignature candidate; player interest spikes.

**Stage 4: High-resolution imaging.** If close enough or interferometer good enough, image the planet directly. Continents, oceans, polar caps, clouds, weather patterns. Spatial information.

**Stage 5: Flyby investigation.** A probe is sent. Single pass, high-resolution data. Magnetic field measured. Higher-resolution imaging. Gravitational field reveals interior. Upper atmosphere sampled if trajectory allows.

**Stage 6: Orbital observation.** Substantial mission. Long-term presence. Climate patterns mapped over a year. Atmospheric chemistry tracked over time. Surface mapped at high resolution.

**Stage 7: Surface investigation.** Landed mission. Direct samples. Ground-based instruments. Walking the surface via EVA or rovers. Ground truth.

**Stage 8: Continued discovery.** Even after a planet is "known," there's more to find. New mission types reveal new things. Time passes and the planet changes. Old data can be re-analyzed with new instruments — a signal recorded years ago and dismissed might turn out to be significant with better tools.

The stages aren't strictly linear. A body might stop at Stage 1 (detected but never investigated). A flyby might skip Stages 1-4 if those instruments weren't used from home. Higher-stage observations might re-open lower stages when anomalies appear. The catalog tracks each body's current stage and accumulated observations.

### Detection mechanics

Operational gameplay for how detection feels in play.

**Filter switching as a core verb.** When using an instrument, the player selects wavelengths or measurement modes. Switching reveals different aspects of the same object. UI primitive: instruments have a filter selector; switching is a single click.

**The "what is this" puzzle as gameplay.** When an unexpected signature appears, the player figures out what could produce it. Different candidate explanations have different additional signatures. Follow-up measurements disambiguate.

**Distance affects measurements.** A measurement at 100 light-years is much less informative than the same at 10 ly. Balance "investigate from home" against "go closer." Connects detection to mission planning and the laser-sail propulsion tiers.

**Time affects measurements.** Variable stars only reveal their period over time. Atmospheric chemistry varies seasonally. Some signals are transient. Patience is part of the gameplay — a campaign monitoring a variable star takes game-years.

**Multiple investigators see different things.** In multiplayer, different players' instruments might detect different aspects of the same object. Pooling data lets agencies build richer models. Cooperative play is scientifically meaningful, not just socially meaningful.

**Discovery names persist.** Whoever first detects an object gets to name it (filters apply for profanity and trademarks). Names appear in the catalog and persist across agencies. Small detail with disproportionate impact on player attachment.

**The catalog grows.** Every observed object goes into the catalog with all known data and discovery history. Late-game players have thousands of entries. Searchable, filterable, exportable.

**Old mysteries can reactivate.** A signal detected years ago and dismissed might be significant with new techniques. When a new instrument tier unlocks, the catalog can re-analyze old observations and surface new findings.

**Resource detection via spectroscopy.** Detection methods reveal not just that a body exists but estimates of what it contains. Spectroscopy of a body's atmosphere reveals atmospheric composition (oxygen, methane, water vapor, etc.) which constrains likely resources. Spectroscopy of a body's surface (where atmosphere permits) reveals surface composition (iron-rich, ice-rich, silicate, etc.) which constrains accessible resources. Thermal emission reveals subsurface heat which constrains volcanic or tidal-heating activity. Radio emission reveals magnetic field and interior structure.

Resource estimates from remote sensing are probability distributions, not exact quantities. A body that scans as "water-ice rich" might yield substantial water on closer investigation or might be partly silicate dust with surface ice. The estimate gets refined as more detection methods and closer observation tiers (Stage 5+, commit 007) confirm or revise the initial reading.

This is what makes home observatory infrastructure (`### Home observatory`) mission-planning relevant beyond pure discovery. Players use observations to identify likely resource-rich targets, then commit missions to confirm and exploit them.

### Observation as structured activity

**LOCKED:** Observation is a scheduled activity, not a passive readout. The player picks a target, an instrument, a configuration, and a duration. Observations are queued and execute over game-time.

**Components of an observation:**

- **Target selection.** Body, region, coordinates, or specific phenomenon.
- **Instrument selection.** Which observatory instrument or vessel sensor.
- **Wavelength / mode selection.** For multi-mode instruments, filter or measurement mode.
- **Duration / integration time.** Longer integration = lower signal threshold = fainter detections. Shorter integration = faster results.
- **Priority / queue position.** Limited instrument time forces scheduling decisions.

Observations execute over game-time. Some are quick (seconds for bright targets); some take days or weeks (deep integration on faint targets). The player schedules a campaign of observations and works on other things while they complete. Results arrive as transmissions or accumulate in the catalog.

The home observatory (commit 009) provides the player's primary observation infrastructure. Vessel sensors (commit 013) provide observation during missions. Both use the same observation-as-scheduled-activity model.

### Wavelengths and filters as concrete gameplay

**LOCKED:** Each wavelength category from the detection ecosystem (commit 007) has concrete filter and configuration choices that produce different data.

**Visible light (optical):** Filters include broadband (all visible, brightness photometry), narrow filters (specific wavelengths for color information), specific line filters (hydrogen alpha, sodium D, oxygen, methane for atmospheric chemistry). Integration time tradeoff: brighter targets short; faint targets long.

**Infrared:** Mid-IR (heat from cool objects), near-IR (cooler stars, dust-penetrating). Cold-detector requirement for space-based. Reveals: thermal emission, dust, cool stars, exoplanet atmospheres via transit spectroscopy.

**Ultraviolet:** Hot phenomena. Atmospheric absorption above water vapor / oxygen / ozone — requires above-atmosphere observation. Specific UV lines for atmospheric chemistry.

**X-ray:** Hot gas, accretion, stellar coronae. Energy resolution as key parameter.

**Gamma-ray:** Highest-energy phenomena. Transient detection critical.

**Radio:** Frequency selection across MHz to THz. Different physical processes at different frequencies. Reveals: pulsars, masers, neutral hydrogen, planetary radio emission. Lunar far-side advantage. Interferometric baselines.

**Particle / gravitational wave:** Specialized detectors with less filter-switching, more on/off.

Each wavelength has its own UI for configuration, its own decisions, its own data outputs. Layer 1 players (commit 006) accept default filters and get useful results; Layer 4 players configure specific filters and extract more from the same instruments.

### Observation results as interpretable data

**LOCKED:** Observations produce raw data that the player interprets through analysis tools. A spectrum is a graph of intensity vs wavelength. An image is a 2D array of pixels with metadata. A light curve is brightness over time. The player's role in interpreting data is real, not abstracted away.

**Layered engagement (commit 006) applies directly:**

- **Layer 1:** Analysis tools do most of the work. The player sees "Spectrum shows atmospheric composition: 78% N2, 19% O2, 3% Ar" as the result.
- **Layer 2:** The player sees the data alongside the interpretation. Curves are visible. Player can spot when something interesting is happening.
- **Layer 3:** The player uses the data to make predictions. "This absorption feature at 1.4 microns looks like water vapor — I should follow up with deeper integration."
- **Layer 4:** The player works with raw data directly. Builds Vizzy scripts to automate analysis pipelines. Catches features the default analysis missed.

Some observations produce data the analysis tools cannot fully interpret — that's where anomalies emerge. The 90/9/1 distribution rule (commit 008) governs how often unresolved data points resolve to interesting outcomes.

### Anomaly types worth seeding

Categories of phenomena the procgen seeds for the discovery loop. The 90/9/1 distribution rule (section 6) governs how often each resolves to interesting outcomes.

**The transit that shouldn't be.** Periodic dimming with timing that doesn't match a normal exoplanet — too short, too long, irregular. Multiple explanations: weird orbital configuration, transiting structures (rare; megastructure if game allows), instrumentation artifact.

**The chemistry that shouldn't persist.** Atmospheric species chemically incompatible at equilibrium. If both present, something is making them. Most often: unusual geology. Occasionally: biology. Rarely: hand-authored mystery.

**The signal with structure.** Most radio sources are noise or simple sinusoids. A signal with complex temporal structure — modulated, repeated, non-random — is interesting. Usually pulsar interpreted strangely; occasionally more.

**The object that moves wrong.** Proper motion that doesn't match expectations. Could be foreground (close, small) rather than background (far, large). Could indicate a binary system at low angle.

**The gravitational anomaly.** Region where gravitational effects don't match visible mass. Usually mundane (small companion, asteroid family, instrument error). Sometimes: rogue planet, dark matter clump, primordial black hole.

**The variable that varies wrong.** Pattern that doesn't fit known variable star types. Usually: unusual binary configuration. Occasionally: new type of variable.

**The temperature mismatch.** A planet much hotter or cooler than expected for its position. Could be greenhouse, tidal heating, young-age internal heat. Could be something unusual.

**The repeating burst.** A radio source producing strong narrowband bursts. Pulsars do this. Magnetars do this. Possibly other things. Repetition pattern is the key to identification.

**The directional signal.** A transmission whose direction is specific rather than from everywhere. Could be a tightly-focused natural maser. Could be artificial. Investigation determines which.

Each anomaly type has multiple plausible resolutions with weighted probabilities. The player cannot tell from initial detection which category an anomaly falls into — investigation reveals it.

### Anomalies and mysteries

**DEFAULT:** Procedurally seeded anomalies in generated systems. Detected from home/observatory; resolved by going there.

Anomaly examples: unexpected signals, gravitational irregularities, thermal emissions where none should exist, planets in implausible orbits, dust patterns suggesting recent activity, periodic flux from a star.

Each anomaly has a generated 'true cause' the player discovers through investigation. Most are mundane (denser core, recent impact, instrument artifact). Some are unusual (active geology, unexpected composition, biosignature). A few hand-authored mysteries can be seeded into the procedural generation for cross-system narrative threads.

**Anomalies as research questions.** Each detected anomaly opens a research question (see section 5's `### Research as logistics-driven question-answering`). The 90/9/1 distribution (commit 008) governs how often investigation resolves to interesting outcomes. The catalog (commit 009) tracks the question's status from detection through investigation to resolution. Anomaly investigation IS research — same activities, same logistics, same outcomes.

### Interstellar travel: tiered tech progression

**LOCKED:** Three tiers of propulsion technology, each opening qualitatively new gameplay.

#### Tier 1: Sublight rockets

Conventional chemical / nuclear propulsion. Sufficient for in-system exploration. Interstellar travel possible but takes generations of game time — viable for unmanned slow-boat missions, not for live crew.

#### Tier 2: Laser sail propulsion (one-way)

Unlocked through research plus resource investment. Requires construction of a laser array (major infrastructure project, see Bases below).

Mechanics: laser array beams photons at a reflective sail; photon pressure accelerates the spacecraft to fractions of c. Crucially, the sail-ship cannot decelerate at the destination because there is no laser there. Result: tier 2 missions are FLYBY ONLY. Probes pass through destination systems at high speed, gather data, transmit findings home. Probes themselves are lost.

Gameplay: every probe is a meaningful commitment. Information returns, hardware doesn't. Creates real decisions about where to send limited probe capacity.

Payload constraint: sail-launched payloads are small (gameplay-fudged to a few tons, not realistic grams).

#### Tier 3: Laser sail propulsion (two-way)

Requires sending a colony ship via slow-boat sublight propulsion to a destination star. Colony ship carries the materials and crew to build a second laser array at the destination. Multi-decade journey at minimum.

Once destination array is operational, bidirectional fast travel between home and that star becomes possible. Each new star in the network requires its own slow-boat colony expedition first.

This is the structural endgame loop: identify promising star (tier 2), commit colony expedition (tier 3 setup), wait, eventually establish two-way link, repeat.

#### Time dilation effects

All interstellar travel experiences relativistic time dilation. Tier 2 probes are short-duration relativistically (no crew, no return). Tier 3 colony ships experience significant dilation — colonists arrive much younger than the home system has aged. The home system continues to develop during their voyage.

### Transmissions and world communication

**LOCKED:** The world communicates with the player through diegetic radio transmissions, not through modal alert notifications. This is the core mechanism for making the world feel alive without making it feel demanding.

The reference model is Marine Channel 16 — a shared frequency monitored by everyone, used sparingly for things that genuinely concern others, with a discipline of brevity and recognizable signal types. The game's transmissions take the same shape: short, recognizable, only sometimes important.

**Transmissions during active flight.** Ambient radio chatter plays in the background — mission control check-ins, status updates from bases, routine reports from other vessels in the program. Periodic notable transmissions surface specific events. Each transmission has audio and a brief visual indicator (a fading log entry in the HUD corner). The player can attend, ignore, or review later.

**Transmissions persist in a log.** All transmissions, whether attended to or not, persist in a searchable comms log. The world does not lose content because the player was not watching at the moment of transmission. Transmissions that arrived during high warp can be reviewed afterward.

**Differentiated audio/visual signatures by type.** Routine status updates are quiet ambient texture. Notable events have a more present audio cue. Crises are insistent. Players learn to recognize the texture of "something important happened" without reading every transmission. Audio cues should be subtle enough to coexist with active flight focus.

**Configurable attention filtering.** Player settings control which transmission types reach the player's notice and which trigger automatic warp-drop. Some players want every routine update; others want only crises. The world generates all transmissions regardless; player settings filter what is surfaced and what interrupts warp.

**Density and warp handling.** Transmissions fire at game-clock density (one per 5–15 game-minutes of routine play, regardless of warp rate). At high warp, most transmissions silently accumulate in the log without interrupting flow. Only player-configured "important" transmission types (crises by default; player-tunable) automatically drop warp or surface modal feedback. Routine transmissions during warp produce a brief unobtrusive visual feedback (e.g., a transmission counter incrementing cumulatively since last attention), so the player can sense the world is alive without being bombarded. The counter clears when the player drops warp and reviews the log.

The unifying principle: the world has texture and voice; the player chooses what to attend to. Transmissions are the voice. The autonomous evolution of bases, research, crews, and supply lines is the texture. The player's attention is sovereign — the world does not demand engagement, it offers it.

This replaces any earlier "events at calibrated rates" framing as a gating mechanism. The journey-gating job that events were doing is now handled structurally by real-scale interstellar distances in single-player (see section 2 `### Interstellar distances`). Transmissions only need to make the world feel alive, which is a much lighter touch.

In multiplayer, transmissions carry the same role, with the addition of cross-agency broadcasts on the shared Channel 16 frequency for inter-agency texture (see `### Channel 16 broadcasts` below).

**Operational chatter.** Routine radio fills the world with activity without demanding action. Supply craft entering orbit ("Tanker-34567 entering Mars LEO, payload 8 tons hydrogen"). Mission milestones ("Heavy-Lifter-2 cleared for departure from orbital VAB"). Science updates ("Mars Observer photometry session complete, data uploaded"). Engineering updates ("Hydroponics bay 3 running at 94% efficiency this week"). Crew personal touches ("Officer Reyes celebrated 50th birthday aboard Mars Base 2"). Weather and conditions ("Solar weather: minor flare activity, no comms impact"). Status changes ("Research project 'methanogen investigation' awaiting sample return").

Most operational chatter is ignorable. None is critical. But it accumulates into world-feel — the world is populated with activity. The player who pays attention catches details; the player who doesn't gets through.

**Configurable by category:** vessel operations, base operations, mission progress, crew chatter, observatory sessions, external chatter (Channel 16 in multiplayer). Each category independently toggleable. Defaults: chatter logs but does not interrupt warp; transmissions (events worth attention) can interrupt warp per the existing warp-handling rules above.

**Personal entities get chatter.** Named crew get personal touches. Player-named vessels get chatter using their names ("Lucky Strike entering Mars LEO" feels different than "Vessel 34567"). Player-built infrastructure transmits status. This is how attachment forms — players develop relationships with named entities by hearing about them.

The chatter system uses the same underlying transmission infrastructure (density rules, warp handling, Channel 16 routing). It is operational background rather than event-driven; transmissions and operational chatter are different defaults for the same system.

### Channel 16 broadcasts

**LOCKED:** Channel 16 broadcasts are a shared communication channel in multiplayer, carrying transmissions between agencies for ambient inter-agency texture. The cultural layer of how players communicate within the shared universe.

Mechanics:

- Each agency can broadcast on Channel 16. Broadcasts are received by all other agencies, subject to the cross-agency observation-sharing rules from `### Information asymmetry / progressive discovery` (instant within an agency; comms-delayed across agencies in cooperative modes; hidden entirely in competitive modes).
- Discovery announcements are a natural transmission type — when an agency reaches a new observation tier on a body, or resolves an anomaly, the announcement may be broadcast on Channel 16. Other agencies can add the entry to their catalog as third-party data.
- Broadcast scope is configurable. Players choose what their agency broadcasts. Open-science play broadcasts everything; competitive play broadcasts only what is strategically advantageous to share.
- Broadcasts follow the same density and warp-handling rules as other transmissions (see above). At high warp, broadcasts accumulate in the log; only player-configured "important" types interrupt warp.

In single-player, the agency has no peers to broadcast to and Channel 16 carries only the agency's own internal comms (mission control, base reports, vessel chatter). The mechanism is the same; the audience is the agency itself.

**Discovery announcement transmissions.** When an agency reaches a new observation stage on a body, or resolves an anomaly, or finds something noteworthy, the agency can broadcast on Channel 16. The broadcast includes the discovery name (if named), the body's current observation stage, headline findings. Other agencies receive the broadcast and can add the entry to their catalog as third-party data. Configurable broadcast scope: open-science play broadcasts everything; competitive play broadcasts only strategic findings. Broadcast density follows the existing transmission density rules — at high warp, broadcasts accumulate in the log; only configured "important" types interrupt warp.

### Home system evolves autonomously

**LOCKED:** The home system continues to exist and develop while the player's attention is on other vessels, other systems, or interstellar transits. Autonomous evolution is constrained to prevent passive accumulation from creating optimization pressure: long absences change *who* and *what*, not *how much*.

**What evolves autonomously (narrative texture):**

- Events fire procedurally from a designed pool, weighted by colony state. Events that occurred while the player was engaged elsewhere are communicated via transmissions when the player returns to attention (see `### Transmissions and world communication` above).
- Crew age, relationships develop, characters live their lives.
- Inter-agency political dynamics shift over time at a high level (mechanics deferred to Phase 7+).

**What does not evolve autonomously (would create optimization pressure):**

- Bases don't accumulate resources passively beyond their depot capacity. They produce while you are attending; they idle when depots are full. There is no infinite-stockpile reward for placing a miner and leaving for a decade.
- Research doesn't auto-progress without active prioritization. A research project advances when assigned scientists are working on it; abandoned projects pause.
- Population growth is bounded by habitat capacity and life support, not arbitrary time.

**Supply line refinement.** Depot capacity caps local accumulation. Supply lines redistribute resources within the base network but don't create infinite total storage — the network has finite total capacity, equal to the sum of depot capacities. Supply lines let producing bases keep producing past their own depot caps by shipping to consuming bases or to depot bases with available capacity. Supply lines have finite throughput; redistribution is rate-limited, not instantaneous. This prevents a single tiny depot from functionally becoming infinite storage by being constantly drained.

**The principle: long absences change who and what, not how much.** Returning from a 50-year journey, the player finds the base they left — same modules, same depot levels (likely depleted), same physical footprint. But the people are different, the political situation has shifted, the log of events that happened is rich. The material world is recognizable; the human world has moved on. This is the *Interstellar* emotional structure made structural rather than aspirational.

The depth is bounded for v1: constrained numerical evolution plus procedural events from a designed pool. A richer version (autonomous colony decisions, evolving politics with mechanical depth, emergent factions) is a Phase 7+ design question, not a v1 commitment.

In multiplayer, autonomous evolution is lighter because other players' presence does the work of making the world feel alive. Same systems, different prominence.

### Physics-driven gameplay

**LOCKED:** Six categories of physics-driven gameplay are integrated as substrate, not features. Each produces gameplay consequences across procgen, observation tiers, mission planning, tech tree progression, and emergent narrative. These are not separate systems bolted on; they are how the game's universe works.

**Stellar physics.** Stellar type affects available energy (red dwarf shifts spectrum redder, specialized infrared collectors needed; blue giant produces enormous UV/X-ray, damages electronics). Stellar variability matters: flare stars periodically emit radiation bursts that can sterilize surfaces or fry electronics. Stellar age affects what's there: young systems are chaotic, old systems are settled. Stellar mass determines habitable zone location and characteristics: red dwarf habitable zones are close-in, producing tidally-locked planets with terminator-zone gameplay.

**Magnetic phenomena.** Planetary magnetic fields protect atmospheres and surfaces. Detected via magnetometer during flyby. Tells whether long-term colonization is viable. Magnetospheres produce auroras: gameplay-relevant beyond looking pretty (indicate magnetic activity, interfere with radio communications near poles, show where the field is strongest for radiation shielding). Stellar magnetic activity produces interference: sunspots and coronal mass ejections scramble communications during stellar storms. Magnetar fields create no-fly zones: extreme magnetic neutron stars destroy electronics from light-years away.

**Radio and electromagnetic phenomena.** The richest single source of immersion-driven gameplay. Characteristic radio signatures: pulsars (periodic radio pulses detectable at galactic distances), quasars, galactic background sources. Hydrogen 21cm emission reveals interstellar gas distributions. Radio interference is gameplay: stellar activity, magnetic storms, nebula crossings affect communications. SETI-style anomalies are the dream content: coherent signals from unexpected sources, most resolving mundane, a few genuinely unexplained.

**Gravitational phenomena.** Gravitational lensing reveals invisible massive objects: black holes, dense neutron stars, rogue planets. Tidal forces affect planets near massive bodies: moons near gas giants experience tidal heating that drives volcanism. Gravitational wave events are detectable transients: neutron star mergers or black hole collisions register as signals. Rogue planets are detectable only by gravity.

**Atmospheric and chemical phenomena.** Oxygen is a biosignature candidate: free oxygen is chemically reactive and shouldn't persist unless something produces it. Methane suggests active geology or biology. Methane-oxygen disequilibrium is a stronger biosignature than either alone. Specific atmospheric features (clouds, polar caps, weather patterns) are visible from telescope distance. Resource distribution follows from chemistry and geology: hydrogen comes from gas giants and water ice; heavy metals from worlds with major impacts; volatiles from young or cold worlds.

**Time and history phenomena.** Light from distant stars is old: a star 100 ly away — the light is 100 years old. The star might have already exploded. Supernova gameplay: detection systems pick up light first, then neutrino burst (which arrives before light because neutrinos don't interact with intervening matter), then expanding remnant becomes visible. Players can race to a recently-exploded star to study the aftermath. Stellar evolution affects which systems have what: old systems have evolved stars; young systems have main-sequence and active formation.

**How these integrate:** Physics-driven systems are not separate features. They thread through procgen (the 14-stage pipeline produces physics-coherent output; stellar context cascades through all subsequent stages), through observation (each tier reveals additional physics-relevant information), through tech tree (better magnetometers detect weaker fields; better spectrographs resolve finer spectral lines), through transmissions (physics-driven content: "Stellar flare activity rising, expect comms degradation"), and through mission planning (physics-driven hazards become real constraints).

### Home observatory

**LOCKED:** The home observatory is the player's primary discovery infrastructure. The player builds it out over time, makes meaningful choices about what to invest in, and unlocks qualitatively different categories of discovery with each investment. This is the one area where optimization gameplay is genuinely on-theme — capability optimization, not resource optimization.

The minimal-tycoon positioning (section 1) applies: resources serve gameplay decisions, not economic optimization. The home observatory is the explicit exception because optimizing it directly serves the discovery loop. Players are not trying to make more money; they are trying to see more of the universe.

**Player choices:**

*What to build and where.* Ground-based observatories on the home planet (cheap, atmospheric limits apply). Space-based observatories (mid-game capability, all wavelengths accessible). Lunar far-side observatories (high-tier, radio-quiet). Each location has tradeoffs.

*Which instruments to build.* Optical, infrared, radio, X-ray, gamma-ray, gravitational wave, particle detectors. Each opens a category of discovery. Bigger optical sees fainter objects; spectroscopy reveals chemistry; radio opens entirely new objects invisible in optical; space-based opens UV/X-ray/gamma-ray.

*Whether to build interferometers.* Combining multiple instruments improves resolution dramatically. Two observatories on opposite sides of the home planet are an interferometer. Adding space-based and lunar instruments extends the baseline. Real (radio interferometry is well-established) and pays off long-term investment.

*Maintenance and servicing.* Space-based observatories require servicing over time. Components fail. The player's program has to maintain capability or watch it decay.

**Integration with existing systems:**

- *Tech tree:* Observatory upgrades are tech tree nodes with prerequisites.
- *Resource system:* Building observatories consumes resources. Larger instruments require more.
- *Base system:* Observatories are base modules with the same architectural model as other base modules.
- *Research:* Observatory research generates results over time, deterministically progressing toward instrument unlocks. Consistent with bounded autonomous evolution — research advances when scientists are assigned.
- *Mission planning:* Each observation campaign is a small mission. Schedule the observation, point the instrument, wait for results, receive transmission, update catalog.

The home observatory is the mid-to-late-game system. By Phase 7, when the galaxy is open to interstellar exploration, the observatory is what the player has been building for hours. Their catalog is what their observatory has produced.

**First-image moments are designed memorable events.** When a space-based observatory first comes online and begins returning images, the first image is a designed moment. The transmission ("First-light from [observatory name]: image of [target] received") is high-priority. The image is presented full-screen with the catalog entry it updates. The player gets a beat to recognize what they're seeing.

First-image moments repeat across major instrument unlocks — first space-based telescope returning a high-resolution planet image, first infrared array finding a hidden cool object, first radio array picking up a previously-unknown signal. Each is a "the reach" dramatic shape (`### Dramatic shapes the game produces` in §1) cashed out as a specific event. The commitment is that these moments get more weight than routine observation results.

### Multiplayer as shared universe

**LOCKED:** Multiplayer is structurally a shared universe with shared game-time. All players exist in the same physical universe at the same moment. Players can physically interact — dock with each other's ships, meet at colonies, conduct joint missions — because they share both time and space.

**Shared time multiplier with player coordination.** Players collectively run the simulation at an agreed rate. The rate can range from 1× (real-time for precision flight) up to very high multipliers (for compressing long journeys). All players experience time at the same ratio. When one player needs precision flight, they request the group lower the rate. When all players want to fast-forward, they collectively raise the rate. This is normal multiplayer coordination — players adapting to each other's needs in real-time. The architecture supports any rate; the choice is a player-facing decision the group makes together. For competitive play, the rate may be host-controlled or rule-bound rather than purely consensus.

**Interstellar travel in multiplayer uses spatial warp.** The same propulsion tiers as single-player exist (tier 1 sublight, tier 2 laser sail one-way, tier 3 laser sail two-way) and serve as tech gates — without the unlocked propulsion tech, interstellar is not accessible. But in multiplayer, propulsion at interstellar tiers operates as spatial warp rather than relativistic transit: the vessel traverses interstellar distance at a configured warp velocity for its tier, without invoking real relativistic physics.

**No time-dilation in multiplayer.** Crew on multiplayer interstellar vessels do not experience proper-time dilation. Their clocks track game-time. They age at the same rate as the universe.

The reasoning for this design choice: relativistic time-dilation creates frame-dependent time, which fundamentally conflicts with shared-time multiplayer. Every workaround introduces structural complexity that hurts gameplay. The cleaner solution is to acknowledge that time-dilation is a single-player design feature and that multiplayer interstellar uses a different mechanism. Both modes share most of the design — physics, propulsion tiers as tech gates, base building, exploration, tech progression — and differ only in how interstellar transit operates.

**Same systems, different behavior by mode.** The three-mode physics architecture (PhysX-active, Kepler-rails, interstellar-cruise) is preserved. The interstellar-cruise mode handles both relativistic transit (single-player) and spatial warp (multiplayer); the game mode and propulsion tier determine behavior. The physics modes themselves are unchanged; the mode behavior is parameterized.

**Cooperative play** is full shared-universe interaction — joint missions, shared bases (player decides whether to share their bases or run them privately), docking, rescue operations, coordinated exploration.

**Competitive play** can include direct physical interaction (races where ships are racing in shared space), shared-resource competition (multiple players targeting the same asteroid belt), or milestone competition (first to discover, first to establish). All within the shared universe.

**Issue #10 (time-warp authority in multiplayer) is resolved by this architecture.** The "whose clock wins" problem dissolves because all players share the same time at any moment; the rate of time advancement is a coordinated decision; propulsion is mode-aware. There is no clock to authoritatively own across players because the clock is shared by construction.

**Architecture compatibility:** the shared-universe model operates on top of the commit `002` determinism boundary. Authoritative state outside the PhysX-active envelope is deterministic and replicates cleanly across players. PhysX-active vessel state is host-authoritative per the commit `002` model (the host runs PhysX for nearby-physics vessels; followers interpolate received state). This subsection does not change the determinism boundary; it specifies the gameplay model that runs on top of it.

## 5. Resources, bases, and logistics

This system serves the space game, not the other way around. Keep it tactile, keep it light, keep the engineering complexity in the space part.

### Resource set

**LOCKED:** Eleven physical resources at v1 plus categorical samples. Helium-3 and antimatter are Phase 7+ additions.

**Fuel and propellant:**

- **Hydrogen** — high-Isp propellant, fuel cell input. From gas giants, water ice cracking.
- **Oxygen** — propellant oxidizer and crew life support (combined). From water electrolysis or atmospheric processing on oxygen-rich worlds.
- **Methane** — chemical fuel, cryogenic storage required. From rocky worlds with biology or geological methanogenesis, gas giant atmospheres.
- **Kerosene** — chemical fuel, storable at room temperature. Lower Isp than methane, better density, simpler engineering tradeoff.
- **Xenon** — ion drive propellant. Trace gas in atmospheres; expensive to extract; strategic for high-Isp low-thrust missions.

**Life support:**

- **Water** — crew life support, radiation shielding, electrolysis feedstock. Ubiquitous as ice; recyclable.
- **Food** — calories. Rations or hydroponics.

**Power:**

- **Fissile material** — nuclear reactor fuel and propulsion. Multiple isotopes (U-235, Pu-239, U-233, Th-232) aggregated. Rare, strategic.

**Materials:**

- **Structural metals** — iron, aluminum, titanium aggregated. Hulls, structural members, base construction. Available almost everywhere.
- **Rare metals** — copper, nickel, cobalt, platinum group aggregated. Electronics, advanced components, specialty applications. Specific bodies, often asteroid-sourced.
- **Rare earths** — lanthanides plus a few others. Highest-tech applications. Strategically located, gating for specific tech.

**Special:**

- **Research samples** — categorical inventory (not fungible). Specific samples from specific places gate specific tech unlocks. Different gameplay shape than the other resources.

**Phase 7+ additions:**

- **Helium-3** — fusion fuel. Lunar regolith, gas giant atmospheres.
- **Antimatter** — highest energy density. Produced in dedicated facilities at enormous energy cost.

**Multi-use principle.** Every resource has multiple sources and multiple uses. Water serves life support, shielding, and propellant feedstock. Oxygen serves life support and oxidizer. Hydrogen serves propellant and fuel cell. Each resource has interesting decisions about *where* to source it.

**Direction B chemistry (not full simulation).** Resources are discrete gameplay-meaningful units with realistic uses and sources, not simulated element-by-element chemistry. Electrolysis is "spend electricity to convert water to hydrogen and oxygen at fixed efficiency," not a chemical reaction simulation.

### Base structure

**LOCKED:** Bases are sites (chosen locations) with modules (functional buildings). No architectural construction. No wall placement.

Module types:

- Habitat (houses crew, consumes life support)
- Miner (extracts a resource from the body; rate depends on body's resource richness)
- Refinery / gas processor (converts one resource type to another)
- Storage (VAB-resident; resources buffer in VAB capacity per `### Supply lines`)
- Power (solar, nuclear, etc.; module operation requires power)
- Research station (advances research projects; specialized stations give location bonuses for relevant tech)
- Laser array (the tier 2/3 infrastructure; massive resource and assembly cost)
- Shipyard / construction (assembles vessels from delivered resources, eventually allowing off-world manufacturing)

Modules have resource inputs, outputs, and power requirements. Player decisions are 'what modules to build where' and 'how to connect them via supply lines,' not 'where to put each wall.'

### Supply lines

**LOCKED:** Supply lines operate as scheduled real vessels rather than abstract throughput parameters. Each route consists of a launch site, a destination, a configured supply craft design, and an automated schedule (time-based, threshold-based, or conditional via Vizzy). Routes execute autonomously, launching the supply craft when conditions are met, executing the trajectory, delivering payload at destination, returning if applicable.

Storage exists in VAB facilities; there are no separate depot modules. The home VAB is starting storage at the home planet; orbital VABs and surface VABs at other bodies provide storage at their locations. The supply craft are vessel designs like any others, with full engineering trade-offs (payload fraction, propulsion choice, reliability margin). The word "depot" is sometimes used colloquially in this doc and in player-facing text to mean "the storage at a facility"; that usage is consistent with VAB-as-storage rather than referring to a separate Depot module.

**Route components:**

- **Launch site:** any facility with launch capability.
- **Destination:** any facility that can receive payload.
- **Supply craft design:** a vessel design (player-built or default-suggested) configured for the route.
- **Schedule:** time-based ("launch every N days at next transfer window"), threshold-based ("launch when destination resource X falls below Y%"), event-based ("launch when manually triggered"), or conditional (Vizzy-scripted).
- **Active/paused state:** routes can be paused without losing configuration.

**Default routes are easy to set up.** When the player establishes a route, the game suggests a basic craft design procedurally generated from the route's requirements, a basic schedule (time-based at the next regular transfer window), and a basic trajectory (Hohmann transfer or similar standard profile). The player can accept defaults for working logistics in seconds, or customize each element for efficiency.

**Routes can fail.** A supply craft may fail at launch (insufficient fuel margin, mechanical failure), in transit (collision, navigation error), or at destination (insufficient delta-v for arrival). Failures cascade through dependent operations and surface as transmissions. The route pauses pending player attention; cascading consequences propagate to anything depending on that supply.

**Network capacity emerges from VAB storage plus active routes.** The total storage in a player's network is the sum of VAB capacities. Routes don't create additional storage; they redistribute resources between VABs. A producing facility whose VAB is full and whose downstream consumers are also full will idle (per the bounded-autonomous-evolution rules in section 4's `### Home system evolves autonomously` subsection).

**Visualization on the map.** Active supply routes are visualized on the map at the appropriate scale. Players can see their logistics network at a glance, sort routes by status, identify failed or paused routes, and access route management from the map view.

**Cost accounting is real engineering.** Supply craft consume fuel to deliver fuel. Routes have efficiency — a route that uses 80% of its payload mass on getting there is mostly burning fuel rather than moving it. Players who design efficient supply craft have efficient logistics. The rocket equation discipline applies — long-range supply is genuinely expensive.

**Route progression:** first-time players get working routes from defaults. Engaged players optimize craft designs. Mastery players automate with Vizzy and build sophisticated multi-route logistics networks. Same system, different depths.

**Strategic depth in supply route design.** Beyond default routes (commit 011b), engaged players design routes with significant strategic variation:

- **Return-trip cargo:** Supply ships return loaded rather than empty (lab samples from base to home; mined ore from outer system to processing).
- **Multi-stop routes:** Single supply craft visits multiple bases on one trip, dropping cargo at each.
- **Gravity-assist trajectories:** Long-haul supply routes use planetary gravity to reduce fuel cost, accepting longer transit time for efficiency.
- **Refueling cascades:** Routes that refuel intermediate stations enable longer-range routes downstream.
- **Synchronized launches:** Multiple supply craft launched together, splitting at staging points.
- **Specialized cargo handlers:** Bulk-water tankers, fissile-material cargo (with shielding mass cost), cryogenic methane tankers (each with parts vocabulary optimized for the specific cargo).

These are emergent strategies, not designer-authored mission types. The vessels-on-schedules model supports them; players who optimize their logistics discover them.

**Supply unit measurement per resource type:**

- **Tons** for bulk materials: food, water, structural metals, methane, kerosene, oxygen (when stored cryogenically in bulk).
- **Kg** for high-value or low-mass: xenon, fissile material, rare earths, rare metals (when stored in mission-grade quantities).
- **Categorical inventory** for research samples (not measured in mass; specific samples from specific places).
- **Kg or specific volume units** for atmospheric gases when measured for life support purposes.

Resource amounts in mission planning and supply route configuration display in their appropriate units. Conversion between unit types (e.g., kg of fissile to tons of fuel mass equivalent for mass-budget calculations) is automatic where needed.

**Auto-route complexity acknowledgment.** Some auto-supply routes are exceptionally difficult to set up because of planetary motion — interplanetary supply routes have transfer windows that occur every few months to a few years; routes that have to thread through multiple transfer windows in sequence can be very difficult to optimize. The player handles this complexity directly when setting up such routes (sometimes with Vizzy script assistance). The design accepts this difficulty because alternative paths exist — for routes that are too hard to set up, easier-but-slower alternatives are available (direct Hohmann transfers without optimization; manual launches per cycle). The discipline: enough easy options exist that the player can always find a workable route; complex optimization is rewarded but not required.

### Research as logistics-driven question-answering

**LOCKED:** Research is the structured investigation of specific questions, not abstract progression through a tech tree. Questions arise from gameplay (anomalies detected, mission data, catalog entries with unresolved status, engineering challenges). Answering questions requires specific activities — observations, missions, sample analysis, equipment tests. The activities have logistics costs (vessels, fuel, crew time, instrument time, sample requirements). Completing the activities resolves the question and produces outcomes (capabilities unlocked, catalog entries closed, follow-up questions opened).

**Two question categories:**

**Discovery questions** arise from the universe: "Why is this body's atmosphere in chemical disequilibrium?" "What's producing this radio signal?" "Why is this star's spectrum unusual?" Discovery questions emerge from anomalies (commit 007), observation results (commit 014's section 4 additions), or unresolved catalog entries. Investigation requires specific missions, observations, or sample returns.

**Engineering questions** arise from the player's program: "Can we improve nuclear thermal Isp by 10%?" "Will this hull material survive Venus atmospheric entry?" "Is this propellant combination stable over a decade?" Engineering questions emerge from design needs and infrastructure goals. Investigation requires equipment tests, simulations, prototype builds, and laboratory work.

**Research project anatomy:**

Each project has a stated question, required activities, identified costs, expected duration, and outcomes on completion. The player commits resources to the project; activities execute; the project resolves.

*Required activities* are specific. "Investigate disequilibrium chemistry on body X" might require: an atmospheric sampling probe mission (cost in vessel, fuel, time), sample return mission (cost in vessel, return-vehicle, time), laboratory analysis on home (research module time, samples consumed), follow-up observation campaign (observatory time on specific wavelengths). Each activity is real gameplay work, not abstract resource expenditure.

*Logistics costs* connect research to the rest of the design. Research requires vessels (the missions). Vessels require fuel and crew (supply lines). Laboratory analysis requires research modules (base infrastructure). Some samples are categorical inventory (commit 011b's resource list) — specific samples from specific places are required for specific projects.

*Outcomes* on completion: tech capabilities unlocked (improved engines, better sensors, advanced manufacturing), the question's catalog entry closed (status changes from "open" to "resolved"), follow-up questions opened (new investigations enabled by what was learned).

**The tech tree as questions and answers.** What was previously called the tech tree is now the accumulated set of resolved engineering questions. "Nuclear thermal engines" is not an abstract node — it's "we ran the development program: tested fuel pellets, validated structural materials, built and tested a prototype. We now have nuclear thermal capability." The unlocks are real because the work was real.

Some engineering questions can be answered through deliberate development without any prior anomaly. Other questions require investigation to arise — you didn't know to ask about magnetar shielding until you encountered a magnetar.

**Bounded autonomous progression preserved.** Research advances only when scientists are assigned to projects. Abandoned projects pause; reassigning resumes. This is the same bounded-evolution rule (commit 004c) applied to the question-answering model: questions advance toward resolution when work is being done on them.

**Research stations have location bonuses (preserved from prior locked content).** Solar physics research is faster near a star, materials research is faster on a unique-geology moon, biological research is faster on a body with biosignatures. The location-bonus mechanic ties exploration to research — putting research infrastructure where it can do its work efficiently is a player decision.

**The catalog (commit 009) becomes the research database.** Every body's catalog entry includes its open questions and resolved questions. The catalog is searchable by question status (find all open questions about atmospheres; find all questions related to body X; find unresolved questions that would unlock specific capabilities if answered).

**Research configurable at game creation (commit 010).** A player who wants everything available from start sets "Progression source: neither — everything unlocks immediately" and skips research as a gating mechanism. A player who wants progression keeps the default — research is the gate, questions are the work.

**Why this reframe matters.** Research is no longer parallel to discovery; it IS discovery, executed deliberately. The same activities (observations, missions, sample returns) that produce discovery also produce research outcomes. The tech tree is no longer abstract grinding — it's the accumulated record of what the player has investigated and learned. The progression feels meaningful because every advancement traces back to specific work the player did.

### Mass and delta-v as central currencies

With resource economics, every kilogram of payload has an opportunity cost in fuel mass. This makes mass budgets meaningful in a way KSP made optional. Players who don't want to think about it can build oversized rockets and waste resources. Players who do want to think about it can optimize and get more done with less. Both work; the latter is rewarded.

### Life support model

**LOCKED:** Three-category architecture for crew sustenance and exposure.

**Consumption resources (continuous).** Food, water, oxygen consumed per crew per day at fixed rates. Production via hydroponics, recyclers, generators. Storage as consumables. Crafts and bases have running totals.

**Exposure tracking (cumulative).** Radiation dose, zero-G exposure, mission stress. Accumulate over time with thresholds. Mitigated by shielding (mass cost), spin gravity (engineering cost), mission rotation (logistics cost).

**Failure modes (discrete).** Decompression, thermal failure, G-force injury, radiation event, supply depletion. Fire when conditions met.

**Difficulty toggles:**

- **Off:** No life support requirements.
- **Abstract (default):** Single life support resource consumed per crew per day.
- **Full:** All three categories tracked separately, realistic tolerances.
- **Brutal:** Adds psychological stress, individual crew health tracking.

Architecture lands in Phase 2 (body parameter set supports it). Full implementation in Phase 6 with the resource system.

**G-force as a fourth exposure category (alongside radiation, zero-G, mission stress).** Sustained or peak G-force exceeding a crew member's tolerance causes injury — incapacitation rather than instant death in normal cases. Brief overlimits cause transient stress; sustained overlimits cause critical condition. G-force is real telemetry (`### Movie-moment mechanics` in §3) that the player can monitor; warnings issue as crew approach their tolerance limits.

**Life support failure flavor.** Specific failure modes for life support produce specific log entries and alert framing:

- **Food contamination.** Hydroponics or storage failure makes food inedible. Crew face supply depletion within days. Solution: emergency resupply, rationing, or evacuation.
- **Water loss.** Storage failure (boiloff, leak) drops available water below threshold. Solution: emergency resupply or restricted use.
- **Atmospheric failure.** Decompression, contamination, scrubber failure. Solution: depending on severity, EVA suit operation, emergency seal, or evacuation.
- **Radiation event.** Stellar flare or local source exceeds shielding capacity. Specific crew enter critical condition based on individual tolerances and shielding location.
- **Thermal failure.** Heating or cooling system fails. Habitable temperature range exceeded. Solution: repair, evacuation, or thermal management until repair.

Each failure produces a transmission with specific framing (not a generic "life support failure" alert) and a log entry so the player can review what happened.

**Crew critical condition mechanics.** When a crew member exceeds tolerance limits in any exposure category, they enter critical condition. Critical-condition crew cannot perform tasks. Healthy crew on the same vessel or base can continue operations and care for the patient. Critical condition is recoverable through return to a base with medical capability — full heal on arrival, provided the player reaches the base within the cause-specific window.

**Cause-dependent recovery thresholds.** Different failure modes have different windows for successful recovery:

- **Acute radiation exposure:** Tight window. Hours to a couple of days. Requires rapid rescue.
- **Decompression / atmospheric failure:** Tight window. Minutes to hours, depending on the survivability of immediate response (EVA suit, emergency seal).
- **G-force injury:** Moderate window. Days. Critical condition stabilizes but requires medical attention.
- **Thermal failure (heat or cold injury):** Moderate window. Hours to days.
- **Supply depletion (food, water):** Longer window. Days to weeks. Crew survive without resupply for limited time but can hang on longer than under acute trauma.
- **Zero-G or mission stress exceedance:** Long window. Mostly chronic; not acutely fatal. Recovery is gradual.

If the player fails to reach a base within the cause-specific window, critical condition can become death. The specific thresholds are balance parameters to be tuned during Phase 5; the architectural commitment is that windows are cause-dependent rather than uniform.

This produces gameplay around crew rotation and rescue urgency: when a crew member becomes critical from acute radiation, the player has hours to launch a rescue. When the cause is supply depletion, the player has more time but still finite. Differential endurance (above) means specific crew matter — losing the only veteran qualified for high-G operations affects program capability.

**Self-repair mechanics with two-tier difficulty.** Modules can fail in ways that are repairable in-place:

- *One healthy crew + correct supplies.* The healthy crew member spends time repairing the failed module. Supplies (parts inventory at the location, or supplies shipped in) are consumed. Repair takes hours to days depending on module severity. The crew member doing repair can't do other tasks during this time.
- *Two healthy crew + presence at location.* Two crew members can repair without supply consumption — they have the engineering knowledge between them to improvise. Still takes time (the crew aren't simultaneously doing other tasks): hours to days based on module severity. Minor instrument failures take a few hours; major life support failures take days. Time advances normally during repair; the player can time-warp through it. Still requires that they be present at the failure location.

Naturally constrained by routing. Even with two people available, getting them to the failure location takes effort — manual routing if it's not a pre-flown path. This incentivizes keeping bases populated (self-sufficient crew handles routine failures) and produces real rescue opportunities when a remote site fails with insufficient crew.

**Abandoned craft and bases decay.** Vessels and bases left without crew gradually accumulate failures. Decay timescale is decade-scale — a base abandoned for one year is mostly fine; a base abandoned for ten years has lost most non-critical instruments and has multiple module failures. Bringing two healthy crew restores full functionality (per above auto-repair rule); bringing one with supplies restores progressively. This produces "the legacy" dramatic shape (`### Dramatic shapes the game produces` in §1) — abandoned outposts that need rehabilitation, derelict ships in distant orbits that can be recovered.

## 6. Procedural generation

### Scope

**LOCKED:** Procgen produces all planets in the universe, including the home system. The home system is hand-tuned — its bodies are generated by the same procgen pipeline that produces galaxy-beyond bodies, but with parameters chosen deliberately rather than seeded randomly. "Hand-crafted home system" means hand-tuned procgen parameters per home-system body plus optionally hand-placed named landmarks (impact craters, mountain ranges, landing sites) on the procedural terrain. The procgen pipeline is one system; the difference between home-system and galaxy-beyond bodies is whether their parameter sets are designer-chosen or seed-derived.

Home system: tutorial, soul, screenshots. Hand-tuned parameters per body. Every body designed deliberately. Learning curve choreographed.

Galaxy beyond: sparse star distribution, each star seeds a system; system bodies are procgen-generated with seed-derived parameters.

### Generation architecture

**LOCKED:** The procgen architecture is a hierarchy of generation layers, each deterministic from its seed and the parameters supplied by parent layers. The full universe exists in the master seed; specific output is computed on demand and cached.

**Six-layer hierarchy:**

- **Layer 1 — Galactic structure.** Master seed defines the playable region's shape, stellar density distribution, large-scale structure (clustering, sparse corridors, the equivalent of stellar associations). Statistical, not a star list.
- **Layer 2 — Star sampling.** Specific stars sampled from the galactic structure. Each star gets a seed derived from master seed plus position. Output: list of (position, seed) pairs.
- **Layer 3 — Stellar parameters.** Given a star seed and position-derived properties (region affects metallicity, formation history), generate the full parameter set: mass, luminosity, temperature, spectral class, age, magnetic activity, etc.
- **Layer 4 — System composition.** From stellar parameters, generate the planetary system. Number of planets, orbital distances, masses, compositions. Uses real astrophysical heuristics (Hill sphere spacing, frost line, mass distributions).
- **Layer 5 — Body parameters and features.** The 14-stage pipeline (below) generates the full per-body parameter set, terrain, atmosphere, hydrosphere, biomes, resources, special features, anomalies, and detection signatures.
- **Layer 6 — Surface detail.** For any surface location, given body seed plus surface coordinates, generate terrain detail at the requested LOD. Meshes, textures, features the player sees up close.

Each layer is deterministic from its inputs and seed. Two saves with the same master seed produce the same universe. Hierarchical seed derivation: master seed → galactic region → star → system → body → surface. The whole universe exists in the master seed; everything else is computed on demand.

**Lazy generation with caching.** The universe doesn't exist in memory until something looks at it. When a star is first observed (Layer 3 fires). When its planets are detected (Layer 4 fires). When a body is characterized in detail (Layer 5 fires). When the player visits (Layer 6 fires for needed regions). Each layer's output is cached when computed. Save files contain the caches; the universe at large is just the master seed plus the playable-region parameters.

### The 14-stage pipeline (Layer 5)

**LOCKED:** Each body is produced through 14 sequential stages. Each stage takes outputs from earlier stages and produces inputs for later ones. The pipeline is unidirectional. Stages can be developed incrementally; the architecture is fixed; contents can be filled in over time.

1. **Stellar context.** Star mass, luminosity, spectral class, age, magnetic activity, flare frequency. Cascades into everything else. Galaxy-level procgen produces this; the per-body pipeline takes it as input.
2. **Planetary core parameters.** Mass, radius, density, composition (rocky/icy/gaseous/mixed), age, internal structure.
3. **Orbital and rotational parameters.** Semi-major axis, eccentricity, inclination, rotation period, axial tilt, tidal-lock state.
4. **Thermal profile.** Surface temperature ranges, atmospheric gradient, polar vs equatorial differences, seasonal variation. From stellar flux and orbital parameters.
5. **Atmosphere.** Pressure, composition (major and trace species), pressure-altitude curve, weather patterns. From mass, temperature, and composition constraints.
6. **Hydrosphere.** Ocean coverage and composition, ice cap distribution. From temperature range and gravity.
7. **Magnetosphere and geology.** Magnetic field strength, geological activity (volcanism, tectonics). From core composition, rotation, age, tidal heating from nearby massive bodies.
8. **Terrain generation.** Continental patterns, mountain ranges, ocean basins, polar ice. Multi-octave noise parameterized by all prior stages. Quadtree LOD.
9. **Biomes.** Biome distribution across the surface. Visual properties (color, vegetation density, texture, sky color) follow from parameters.
10. **Resources.** Resource deposit placement based on geology. Iron where there's geological activity. Water ice in cold regions. Rare earths in specific geological contexts.
11. **Special feature layers.** Rings, auroras, active volcanism, impact craters, frozen-moment collisions, biosignatures. Each layer is independent with activation rules.
12. **Anomalies.** Discoverable mysteries seeded by system parameters. The 90/9/1 distribution rule governs resolution weights.
13. **Hand-placed features (home system only).** Named landmarks tied to tutorial or narrative.
14. **Detection signatures.** What each detection method would observe. Visible spectrum, infrared, UV, X-ray, radio, magnetic field, gravitational influence, transit characteristics, particle emission, gravitational wave signature where applicable. Computed at body generation time so detection instruments built later read from cached signatures rather than retrofitting bodies.

### Hybrid pipeline-plus-grammar

**LOCKED:** The procgen architecture is pipeline at the macro level (Stages 1-14 produce a body's full parameter set and detection signatures) and grammar at the detail level (within terrain, biomes, and feature placement, rules determine what goes where based on local conditions). Pipeline establishes the big picture; grammar handles local variation.

Grammar is bounded by pipeline. Local rules can't override pipeline-level constraints. A planet whose Stage 2 parameters say "low geological activity" can't have grammar rules that produce active volcanism — the volcanism feature layer wouldn't fire.

### Planet variety tiers

**LOCKED:** Planet variety is delivered in three tiers based on implementation cost.

**Cheap tier (free from the pipeline) — Phase 5-7:** Parameter combinations that emerge naturally. Atmospheric coloration, extreme temperature worlds, tidal locking, wild atmospheric pressures, extreme rotation rates, high-eccentricity orbits, multiple moons, ring systems, binary planet systems, massive ocean wave worlds. Variety is essentially infinite within these categories because parameter space is continuous.

**Medium tier (dedicated systems, 1-3 weeks each) — Phases 5-8:** Binary and multiple-star systems, active volcanism, auroras, comet-like bodies, asteroid belts, pulsars, brown dwarfs, habitable gas giant moons. Each is a discrete project that slots into a session. Ship target: 5-10 medium-tier phenomena by Phase 7 end.

**Hard/hero tier (significant engineering, 1+ month each) — Phase 7-8:** Frozen-moment planetary collisions, Roche-limit destruction, recent collision aftermath, black holes with gravitational lensing, neutron stars with accretion disks, nebulae as flyable regions, megastructures. Ship target at v1: 2-3 hard-tier features.

Volumetric nebulae as flyable regions are also hard-tier (Phase 7+). Implementation involves volumetric rendering, navigation hazards (visibility-limited, particle-density gating sensor effectiveness), aesthetic priority for screenshots and atmosphere. Ship target: 1-2 named nebulae in the playable region at v1 if Phase 7 scope permits; otherwise post-launch.

### Feature-layer architecture

**LOCKED:** Special features (rings, auroras, volcanism, impact craters, frozen-moment collisions, etc.) are implemented as independent feature layers in Stage 11. Each layer has its own activation rule (when does it apply), placement logic, and visual/gameplay effects. Layers compose freely except where explicit anti-correlation rules prevent specific combinations.

Anti-correlation rules prevent chaotic combinations: active volcanism plus heavy rings is dampened (rings would be disrupted); recent major impact dampens stable atmosphere (atmosphere would be disrupted); frozen-moment collision overrides most other features (collision is the defining feature). Rules are tunable parameters, not hard constraints — edge cases are anomalies worth investigating.

**Extreme weather as feature layer.** Some bodies develop extreme atmospheric conditions through specific parameter combinations: high atmospheric pressure plus high axial tilt plus fast rotation can produce planet-spanning storm systems (Jupiter-style banding, super-rotating atmospheric jets, season-long hurricanes). The extreme-weather feature layer activates when the relevant parameter thresholds combine. Visual: persistent storm systems visible from orbit; gameplay: atmospheric flight is genuinely hazardous in these zones, EVA on surface during storms is dangerous, supply route trajectories must account for weather windows.

### Asteroid clusters as system composition variant

**LOCKED:** Some systems contain spatially compact asteroid clusters distinct from the broad ring-shaped asteroid belts that emerge from standard system composition (commit 008 Layer 4). Clusters are tighter groupings — dozens to hundreds of bodies in a small volume, often dynamically related (formed from a recent disruption event, captured into a Lagrange-point cloud, or remnants of a fragmented larger body).

Gameplay distinction: navigating a cluster requires careful trajectory planning at close-quarters scales; mining a cluster yields concentrated resource access; clusters can hide anomalies in their density. The procgen treats clusters as a system composition variant — some systems have one or more clusters in addition to or instead of conventional belts.

Asteroid clusters are medium-tier per `### Planet variety tiers` — dedicated systems work but not as expensive as hard-tier hero features.

### The 90/9/1 anomaly distribution

**DEFAULT:** Of all anomalies and detection results that warrant player investigation, 90% resolve to interesting-but-understandable phenomena (a denser-than-expected core, an unusual atmospheric chemistry with mundane explanation, a planet with striking features that's still "just" a planet). 9% resolve to genuinely surprising results (a true biosignature, a confirmed unusual phenomenon, an unprecedented feature). 1% resolve to once-per-playthrough hero moments (something the player will tell friends about — a megastructure, a frozen collision, an unmistakable artificial signal).

The ratio is what keeps exploration satisfying for hundreds of hours. Each anomaly type has a set of possible resolutions with weighted probabilities. The 1% bucket includes both procedural rare phenomena and hand-authored hero moments. Target: 10-20 hand-authored mysteries at v1, scattered through procgen seeds, each findable once per save.

### Galaxy scope

**LOCKED:** The playable region is a local stellar neighborhood of approximately 10,000-50,000 stars within roughly 100-200 light-years of the home system. Large enough to never be exhausted by any individual player; structured enough to feel approachable rather than infinite. The galaxy at large is visible (stars beyond the playable region are visible through telescopes) but unreachable, with the framing that this is the region currently accessible to humanity given propulsion technology.

### Per-body physics parameter set

**LOCKED:** Every celestial body in the universe — home system, procgen system, every planet, every moon, every asteroid, every star — has a full physics parameter set defined at the time of its first generation. The set includes all parameters required by any system, not just the systems currently implemented. Later phases that add new detection methods, anomaly types, or physics-driven gameplay read from this parameter set rather than retrofitting bodies.

Parameter set at minimum (extensible):

For stars: stellar type (O, B, A, F, G, K, M, brown dwarf, white dwarf, neutron star, etc.), mass, radius, age, luminosity, surface temperature, spectral peak, magnetic activity index, flare frequency, rotation rate, metallicity, system position.

For planets and moons: mass, radius, density, composition (rocky/icy/gaseous/mixed), age, semi-major axis, eccentricity, inclination, rotation rate, axial tilt, tidal-lock state, magnetic field strength, atmospheric composition (per major species, with trace species also recorded), atmospheric pressure profile, surface temperature ranges (polar to equatorial, seasonal), surface gravity, internal heat (residual + radiogenic + tidal), geological activity index, hydrosphere coverage and composition, resource distribution (per resource, with richness), and an extensible "special features" dictionary for things like ring systems, auroras, active volcanism, recent impacts, frozen-moment collisions, biosignatures.

For asteroids and small bodies: subset of planet parameters as relevant, plus orbital family membership.

The parameter set is defined upfront at Phase 2, even though most parameters won't be used until Phase 5, 6, or 7. The reason: adding parameters retroactively is expensive (every existing body in every save needs the new parameters generated). Defining the full set upfront means the seed function can be designed once, and new systems just read parameters that already exist.

Parameters are computed from the body's seed deterministically. Two different game saves with the same seed produce the same parameters. Parameter correlations are part of the spec, not separately tracked: mass and radius produce density; density plus composition produces internal structure; stellar type plus orbital distance plus atmospheric composition produces surface temperature. The seed function enforces these relationships so the output is always physically plausible.


### Resource distribution

Each celestial body has a procedurally generated resource profile (which resources, what richness). Spectroscopy reveals rough estimates; flyby refines; orbital survey confirms; surface investigation gives exact site values. This integrates the discovery mechanic directly into the resource gameplay.

### Tuning is the hard part

Code is 20% of the work. Making procgen output feel distinct and interesting rather than samey is the other 80%. Budget for many iteration passes on parameters and weights.

### Crater primitives and bounded terrain modification

**LOCKED:** The procgen pipeline includes crater placement as a Stage 11 feature layer applicable to all rocky bodies. Initial body generation places craters appropriate to age and bombardment history. The crater system supports runtime additions for impact events, nuclear detonations on surfaces, mining operations, and construction site preparation.

Crater geometry uses standard impact scaling. Energy maps to crater diameter via established impact physics relationships. Surface burst vs subsurface burst produces different geometries. Overlapping craters use standard composition rules.

Each body has a crater list initially populated by procgen but mutable at runtime. Rendering at any LOD reads from the current list. Save state stores the full list including runtime additions.

**Body fragmentation as event-driven state change.** Impacts exceeding the body's gravitational binding energy by sufficient margin trigger fragmentation. The fragmentation event removes the body from procgen and places a procedural asteroid field at the body's orbital position with parameters derived from impact geometry. Small bodies (small moons, asteroids) are fragmentable by realistic gameplay forces. Large bodies (significant moons, planets) require energies beyond normal gameplay range.

Other terrain modification (trenches, roads, biome changes, water displacement, atmospheric effects from impacts) is deferred to post-v1. The crater and fragmentation primitives cover the dual-use technology framework's visible consequences and the dramatic moments produced by engineering gameplay.

**Strip mining as crater primitive subtype.** Mining operations conducted over sustained game-time produce a distinct geometric variant of crater — strip mines are linear/excavated rather than the circular bowl-with-rim shape of impact craters. The strip-mine variant uses the same crater-list-mutable-at-runtime architecture but with a different geometric primitive in the placement system. Both impact craters and strip mines populate the same crater list per body; rendering distinguishes them by their stored geometry parameters.

Strip mines emerge only from player mining operations. They do not appear as starting procgen content — prior-era industrial activity is not seeded as visible strip-mining terrain modification on bodies the player discovers. (Prior-era artifacts and infrastructure are seeded per `### In-media-res starting state` in §1, but those are Tier A/B/C placed-content items, not procedural terrain modification.)

This produces visible long-term consequences of player mining operations. A heavily-mined body looks visibly transformed across decades of game-time. The terrain modification persists across saves per the existing crater architecture.

## 7. UI and information density

Late-game state is enormous: a colony on Tau Ceti, three probes in transit, a research project ongoing, supply lines running, an active mission. The player needs to navigate this without drowning.

### Patterns to use

- **Summary screens:** fleet view, base view, research view, supply line view. Each is a high-density list with sorting and filtering.
- **Alert system:** notifications surface when something needs attention (research completed, supply line broken, anomaly detected, crew running low on supplies). Player chooses which alert types to receive.
- **Pinnable views:** player flags items of interest to track. Map view shows pinned vessels and bases prominently.
- **Map view as primary hub:** the system/galaxy map is where most decisions get made. Active flight is the exception, not the default.
- **Time controls are first-class:** warp up to next event (research complete, vessel arrival, anomaly detection) rather than warping by fixed multipliers.

### First-hour experience

**LOCKED** design pillar: the first hour of play matters more than any other hour.

Target arc for the first hour of a new player:

- Successful launch within 10 minutes (tutorial-guided, pre-built or template craft)
- View of the home planet from orbit within 20 minutes (the awe moment)
- Reach the home moon (Mun-equivalent) within the first hour (the accomplishment moment)
- Reveal of the broader game (anomaly detected, mysterious signal, hint of interstellar) before player puts it down

KSP's first hour was bad — players who stuck with it did so out of stubbornness. Ours must be good. The home system, tutorial, and early game get disproportionate design attention.

### Information legibility

The game tells the player what they need to know. Delta-v indicators, mass margins, projected outcomes, clear failure explanations, predicted trajectories. Difficulty is in the engineering problems, not in the interface.

### Map view hierarchy

**LOCKED:** The game has four primary view scales, each operating at the scale appropriate to player decisions at that scale.

**Galactic / stellar neighborhood view.** Local playable region with all known stars. Stars colored by spectral class, sized by luminosity. Home system highlighted. Distance and direction from home visible. Discovered anomalies marked. Navigation by zoom and pan. Distance scale: light-years. This is where the player makes long-arc decisions: which star to send the next interstellar probe to, where the unexplored regions are.

**System view.** A single star system with planets, moons, asteroid belts, infrastructure, and vessels in the system. Mission planning at the interplanetary scale.

**Body view.** A specific planet or moon at orbital or surface scale. Terrain, weather, infrastructure on the surface, vessels in orbit, biome distribution. Tactical decisions about a specific body.

**Construction view.** The VAB. Local to a specific facility.

Transitions: galactic to system is "select a star, zoom in." System to body is "select a body, zoom in." Body to construction is "enter the facility." Data is consistent across views.

### Catalog as long-term meta-game

**LOCKED:** The catalog of discovered objects is the player's accumulated record of engagement with the universe. Searchable, filterable, sortable, exportable. Late-game catalogs have thousands of entries. The catalog is the meta-game of the long playthrough — it is what the player has been building, and what makes their save uniquely theirs.

**Catalog contents:**

- Every observed object with its current observation stage (0-8)
- Discovery history per object: when found, what instruments detected it, what's been learned, what remains unknown
- Anomaly status per object: unresolved mysteries, follow-up options to resolve them
- Player-assigned tags and notes
- Discovery names (the player's or other agencies' attribution)
- Cross-references to missions, observations, transmissions related to the object

**How the catalog functions as gameplay:**

*Sorting and filtering as exploration planning.* Players sort by anomaly status to find unresolved mysteries; by distance to find accessible targets; by interestingness to find priorities; by body type, stellar context, detection difficulty. The catalog is the discovery-planning interface.

*Old data re-analysis.* When a new instrument tier unlocks, the catalog can re-analyze old observations at the new resolution. Each catalog entry retains the raw observation data; new instruments process it at improved sensitivity. New findings surface as transmissions.

*Catalog as save identity.* Two players with the same procgen seed could have very different catalogs depending on what they investigated and in what order. Their catalogs are unique to their playthrough even when the underlying universe is the same.

*Catalog export and sharing.* Players can export catalog entries or whole catalogs as files. Human-readable format, supports community tooling, follows the same versioning discipline as mode-portable designs. Players share interesting discoveries through whatever channels they prefer. First-party online sharing is not v1 but the format supports it.

*Catalog as Channel 16 broadcast content.* In multiplayer, discoveries are announced on Channel 16. Other agencies receive broadcasts and can add entries to their catalogs as third-party data. Agencies choose to share full data or just announcements.

### Mission Control as primary UI

**LOCKED:** Mission Control is the primary functional UI for the game. It opens on game start after loading screens. It is the entry point for all gameplay activities and the wrapper around the map views.

**Mission Control visual design:**

The Mission Control room presents the player with multiple large TV-style screens. Each screen shows a real-time render of one of the player's vessels at its last save-point position — the render uses the normal flight-view renderer at paused state. A vessel in orbit shows the vessel against the planet below; a vessel on the surface shows the vessel against the landscape; a vessel in deep space shows the vessel against a star background. If a build is in progress in the VAB, one screen shows that build state. If a supply route is active, the relevant supply craft appears on its screen with its current route context.

The default screen count is 6-9 visible at once (specific number tuned during Phase 5 to fit screen real estate at target resolution). Players with more vessels than the default count access the overflow via scroll/page controls — additional screens are reachable by scrolling laterally or by paging through screen sets. Late-game players with dozens of vessels see top-N most recent or most active vessels in the visible set; the full fleet is accessible through the scroll/page controls plus filtering options.

The player navigates between screens using keys ("change channel" navigation) or by direct selection. The TV screens use stylized framing to make the Mission Control aesthetic clear — this is the player's mission control room, not just a vessel list.

**Mission Control panel structure:**

Mission Control includes panels for:

- Vessel TV screens (the main visual element)
- Map view access (entered from Mission Control or from inside flight/VAB views)
- VAB screen (entered for vessels in build/edit state)
- Flight screen (entered for vessels in active flight, KSP-style)
- Simulator screen (described below — sandbox-as-simulator)
- Mission planning panel (active missions, campaigns, supply routes, observatories)
- Catalog panel (accessible from Mission Control directly)
- Settings, save/load

Player clicks a screen or panel to enter that context. Returning to Mission Control pauses the game state.

**Mission Control pauses the game.** While Mission Control is open, simulation time does not advance. The player can spend as long as needed reviewing fleet status, planning missions, switching contexts, without time pressure. This is consistent with the design's commitment to thoughtful play over reflexive play.

**Map view integration.** The map (galactic, system, body — `### Map view hierarchy` in §7) is accessible from Mission Control as a panel/screen, and also from inside flight and VAB views. The map is consistent across all access points — the same map view, the same data, the same controls, just opened from different contexts.

### Sandbox-as-simulator integrated in Mission Control

**LOCKED:** Mission Control includes a simulator screen accessible from game start. The simulator is a sandbox mode for testing and rehearsing without committing real resources. It is not a separate game mode; it is a feature of Mission Control available throughout play.

**Simulator operation:**

Entering the simulator opens a fork of the current game state. The simulator starts from the last save and runs forward with real physics and real craft data. The player can fly any of their vessels, build new craft in the VAB, attempt missions, dock with other craft (real positions per last save), conduct EVA — all using the same systems as the real game.

Exiting the simulator reverts everything. Whatever happened in the simulator (crashes, successful docks, new craft built, fuel consumed) does not persist. The game state returns to the Mission Control state from before the simulator session.

**What the simulator sees:**

The simulator has access only to what the catalog knows. For bodies with landed crew or landed flight computers, the simulator has complete data (real procgen output, real surface, real anomaly content). For bodies that have been scanned remotely (Stage 1-4 in `### Discovery progression — eight stages` in §4), the simulator can fly to them but renders parameter-derived defaults — a sphere with terrain noise from the known seed at known LOD, biome distribution from known atmospheric/temperature parameters, surface featuring matching what would be predictable from the available data. The simulator cannot reveal procgen content beyond what the catalog already knows.

Bodies not yet scanned do not appear in the simulator map. The simulator is honest — it cannot generate knowledge the player doesn't have.

**Stylized simulator UI.** The simulator viewport is framed as a monitor-within-the-game-view: a visible bezel surrounds the gameplay area with "SIMULATOR" prominently displayed on the bezel. HUD elements inside the simulator use distinct visual styling — different button shapes, different colors, different fonts — so the player can recognize sim mode at a glance even during fast play. The framing prevents the player from mistaking a sim session for the real game, particularly on bodies where the simulator renders parameter-derived defaults rather than actual procgen output. The stylization is consistent and persistent throughout the sim session.

**Use cases:**

- Trajectory planning: try a Hohmann transfer or gravity assist before committing real fuel.
- Docking practice: try a complex rendezvous against current real positions before doing it for real.
- Craft testing: launch a new design and see if it survives ascent before risking real launch.
- Mission rehearsal: walk through a long-duration mission's stages to check planning.

Use cases not supported:

- Discovery shortcut: cannot reveal unscanned procgen content.
- Save-scumming: simulator does not advance game state; exiting reverts.

## 8. Difficulty and accessibility

**LOCKED:** Difficulty as independent toggles, not modes. No 'easy / normal / hard' globals.

Players build their own difficulty profile from independent settings:

- Resource scarcity (none / light / standard / hardcore)
- Failure modes (off / cosmetic / functional / brutal)
- Tutorial guidance (verbose / hints / off)
- Delta-v assistance (auto-calculated / manual / off)
- Communication delay (off / scaled / realistic)
- Time-warp limits (generous / standard / strict)
- Mass margins required (none / lenient / realistic)
- Solar system scale (Casual 1/10 / Standard 1/8 / Realistic 1/1, with custom intermediate scales available)
- Life support requirements (off / abstract / full / brutal — see section 5's `### Life support model` for the categorical model)
- Physics fidelity (pragmatic / strict): pragmatic skips some near-tangent SOI grazes for predictor performance; strict applies aggressive detection across all event types. See section 2's Mode transitions and event scheduling for the underlying mechanic.

Each setting is independent. Beginners turn assistance up and consequences down. Veterans turn assistance down and consequences up. Hardcore players brutalize everything. Same game, vastly different experiences. This is the Dwarf Fortress / RimWorld pattern.

### Layered engagement framework

The "layered depth" pillar (section 1) operationalizes as four layers of engagement, each available to any player at any time. The framework is not a difficulty setting — it describes how the same systems serve different player depths simultaneously.

**Layer 1 — Surface experience.** A player who just wants to explore sees beautiful planets, hears about anomalies, and engages with discovery at the level of "this is interesting, I'll go look." The physics is invisible to them — it produces consistent, varied output, but they don't need to know that the red dwarf they're orbiting has more flare activity than a G-type star. Layer 1 is fully sufficient for hundreds of hours of enjoyable play.

**Layer 2 — Visible information.** A player who wants to engage more sees the relevant readings on the UI — magnetic field strength, atmospheric composition, stellar type, surface temperature. They learn what these mean over time, through gameplay context rather than tutorials. The game shows the information; the player chooses whether to think about it.

**Layer 3 — Deductive gameplay.** A player who wants to engage deeply uses the information to make predictions. "This star is M-class with high flare activity, this planet is tidally locked, the terminator zone has stable temperature, that's where I should build a base." This is real reasoning that real planetary scientists do, applied at simplified-game level.

**Layer 4 — Expert play.** The most engaged players push into edge cases. Reading magnetic field variations to find subsurface ocean evidence. Spotting unusual atmospheric chemistry that suggests biological activity. Identifying systems that look mundane but have hidden features. This is the player who builds Vizzy scripts to automate spectroscopic analysis pipelines.

A given player might be Layer 1 for stellar phenomena, Layer 3 for resource extraction, and Layer 2 for everything else. The game doesn't require players to commit; they engage at whatever depth they want, in whatever areas interest them. This framework is the design test for every system: does it provide value at Layer 1 (beautiful, comprehensible) and remain interesting at Layer 4 (rewarding for deep engagement)? Systems that only serve one layer are wrong for this game.

## 11. Working with Cowork

Cowork is a project assistant — it can persist context across sessions, manage files, and act semi-autonomously on tasks. For a project of this scope, treat it as a build collaborator, not just a code generator.

### What to put in Cowork's context up front

- This document. Constraints cascade and Cowork should reject suggestions that violate them.
- A running architecture document that gets updated as decisions are made.
- Phase tracker: current phase, current validation milestone, blockers.
- Decisions log: when an option was considered and rejected, write it down with the reason. Prevents revisiting settled questions.

### How to structure sessions

- Each session has a defined scope tied to one phase. Don't bounce between phases unless explicitly resolving a cross-phase issue.
- Start sessions by re-grounding Cowork in this doc and the phase tracker. Catches drift early.
- Validation milestones are explicit, testable, and binary. Don't move forward until the current milestone passes.
- When Cowork proposes architecture changes, require a written rationale against this doc. If the rationale isn't strong, the existing constraint wins.

### What to be wary of

- AI assistants drift toward making things 'cleaner' by refactoring. Architectural commitments here are deliberate; resist refactors that fight the foundation.
- Don't let Cowork build visuals before Phase 1 is validated. The siren song of pretty screenshots is real.
- If Cowork says something contradicts this doc, the doc wins unless explicitly updated. Update the doc when decisions genuinely change; don't let drift accumulate silently.
- AI tends to add features speculatively ('this might be useful later'). Resist. Build what's in the current phase. Future phases can add what they need.

### Suggested repo layout

- `docs/CONSTRAINTS.md` — this document in markdown.
- `docs/ARCHITECTURE.md` — implementation details that flow from constraints.
- `docs/PHASE_TRACKER.md` — current state, current milestone, blockers.
- `docs/DECISIONS.md` — log of resolved OPEN questions and rejected alternatives.
- `docs/code/<system>.md` — per-system companion docs (see section 12 below).
- `src/` — Unity project source.
- `tests/` — automated tests, especially for foundation systems (orbital mechanics, frame transitions, save round-trip).

## 12. Code companion documentation

This project will be developed with significant AI assistance across many sessions. Companion docs are the project's institutional memory — they keep AI sessions grounded and prevent drift.

### Strategy: docs per system, not per file

**DEFAULT:** One companion doc per major system or subsystem, not per code file.

Per-file docs become stale immediately and create maintenance burden. Per-system docs capture the why and how at the level that actually matters.

### What gets a companion doc

- Each foundation system (CoordinateSystem, ReferenceFrames, OrbitalMechanics, TimeManager, PhysicsModeManager, SaveSystem)
- Each gameplay system (VesselConstruction, FlightControl, ResourceSystem, SupplyLines, ResearchTree, Vizzy, AnomalySystem)
- Each cross-cutting concern (DeterministicRandom, Authority, EventBus, UISummaryFramework)
- Each procgen layer (GalaxyGen, SystemGen, PlanetGen, AnomalyGen)

Some entries in this list are major systems with substantial implementation; others are single utilities or thin patterns. The intent is "one doc per coherent unit of design," not "one doc per item in this list." When utilities are small enough that a per-utility doc would be a few paragraphs and would mostly duplicate context, group them under a `FoundationPrimitives` companion doc (or similar named container per cluster). Cross-cutting concerns at the smaller end (e.g., DeterministicRandom as a utility, the EventBus as a thin pattern) are good candidates for grouping. Major systems (VesselConstruction, OrbitalMechanics, Vizzy) always get their own doc. The grouping decision is made when each doc is started, not in advance; the principle is that the doc should be substantial enough to be worth maintaining and small enough to read in one sitting.

### What a companion doc contains

- **Purpose:** what this system does and why it exists
- **Public interface:** what other systems can call, with intent (not just signatures)
- **Internal architecture:** how it works at a level of detail that lets someone reason about it without reading every line
- **Files:** list of files in this system and one-line descriptions
- **Invariants:** things that must remain true (e.g., 'all orbital state is double-precision, no float anywhere in this system')
- **Cross-references:** which other systems this depends on, which depend on it
- **Known gotchas:** subtle things future-you will trip on
- **Test coverage:** what is tested, what isn't, what the validation milestone is

### Critical practice: doc-driven development

Before writing or substantially changing a system, update its companion doc FIRST. The doc captures intent; the code implements it. If the doc is hard to write, the design isn't clear yet — fix the design before the code.

This is especially important for AI-assisted development. The AI works best when intent is explicit. The companion doc IS the intent.

### Maintenance discipline

- When code changes, the doc gets updated in the same session. Stale docs are worse than no docs.
- When the AI proposes changes, it should reference and update the relevant companion doc.
- Periodically (every few phases), do a doc audit: re-read all companion docs, fix drift, prune obsolete sections.

### Template for a companion doc

Use this consistently across all system docs. Each doc should have these sections: System name and status (in design / implemented / stable / refactoring); Phase it was built in; Last reviewed date; Purpose (why this exists, what it does, what it doesn't do); Public interface (what other systems can call, with intent); Internal architecture (how it works); Files (list with one-line descriptions); Invariants (things that must remain true); Dependencies (what this depends on, what depends on it); Gotchas (subtle things to avoid breaking); Tests and validation (what's tested, what the validation milestone is).

Known traps from prior art in this genre and from AI-assisted projects:

### Project-shape failures

- **Building visuals before foundation.** Beautiful screenshots, broken physics. KSP 2's path.
- **Modeling too many parts before construction is validated.** Procedural parts mitigates this but confirm the system shape with a minimum set first.
- **Scope creep into deep base-building.** Tempting. KSP 2 promised colonies and never delivered the core. Bases serve the space game, not vice versa.
- **Trying to ship multiplayer in v1.** Foundation supports it; feature defers to post-v1.
- **Realism creep.** Adding realism that creates tedium instead of decisions. Apply the test.
- **Engine rewrite temptation at month six.** The fix is almost always working within Unity, not against it.
- **Trying to compete with KSA on raw simulation fidelity.** Different lane. Win on time-dilation interstellar, discovery meta-game, information asymmetry, layered depth.
- **Resource-grinding tedium.** Players who spend hours moving the same hydrogen are bored. Supply lines and Vizzy automation prevent this — design assumes it from the start.

### AI-assistance-specific failures

- **Drift across sessions.** Decisions made in session 5 forgotten by session 20. Mitigated by DECISIONS log and companion docs.
- **Speculative feature additions.** AI adds things 'just in case.' Each addition is decision debt. Build what's in the current phase.
- **Cleanup-driven refactors.** AI sees existing code as suboptimal and rewrites. Sometimes right, often wrong; always disruptive. Reject without rationale.
- **Pattern-matching to wrong analogies.** AI applies generic Unity patterns to a non-generic game. Foundation constraints (doubles, floating origin, determinism) make this game weird. Stay grounded in this doc.
- **Stale doc syndrome.** Code changes without docs updating. Within a few sessions docs are lying. Discipline: update doc in same session as code change.
- **Over-trust of AI architectural judgment.** AI is great at gameplay code, good at well-trodden infrastructure, mediocre at novel foundation architecture. Foundation gets careful human review.
